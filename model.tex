\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Consider a small open economy in which production requires a continuum of
tasks indexed $i \in [0,1]$, ordered by their susceptibility to AI
automation.  Tasks at the low end are easily performed by AI; tasks at the
high end require human judgment and contextual reasoning that AI currently
struggles to replicate.  This ordering maps directly to the
\citet{eloundou2024gpts} exposure scores: a high exposure score indicates that
a large fraction of an occupation's task bundle is susceptible to large
language models, placing that bundle near the low end of the $i$ spectrum.

Final output $Y$ aggregates tasks via a CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ is the quantity of task $i$ used in production.  Each task can
additionally be performed by AI at cost
\begin{equation}
  c^{\mathrm{AI}}(i) = \tau\cdot t(i), \qquad t(i) = e^{\alpha i},\quad\alpha>0,
  \label{eq:ai_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\tau>0$ is an aggregate AI capability parameter (lower $\tau$ reflects
more capable AI) and $t'(i)>0$ ensures that more complex tasks are costlier
to automate.

\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The economy is a price-taker in the world market for $Y$.  The output price
  $P_Y$ is exogenous and fixed.
\end{assumption}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Pre-AI Task Prices}
\label{sec:taskprices}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Before AI, all tasks are performed by human workers.  Competitive firms
minimize cost subject to \eqref{eq:ces}, taking $P_Y$ as given.  The
first-order condition for task $i$ yields the equilibrium task price
(derived in Appendix~\ref{app:foc}):
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = P_Y \cdot \left(\frac{Y}{y_0(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma},
  \label{eq:taskprice_preai}
\end{equation}
where $y_0(i)$ is the pre-AI equilibrium quantity of task $i$.  In
equilibrium, $y_0(i)$ equals the supply of task $i$, which is determined by
the measure of workers whose bundles contain $i$.  Substituting market
clearing into \eqref{eq:taskprice_preai}: tasks supplied by fewer workers
have lower $y_0(i)$, and therefore command higher prices.

\begin{assumption}[Increasing Pre-AI Task Prices]
  The pre-AI task price $p_0(i)$ is strictly increasing in $i$: more complex
  tasks are scarcer (fewer workers supply them) and are priced higher.
\end{assumption}

\noindent This assumption is consistent with the empirical college wage
premium: tasks in the upper range of $i$ --- disproportionately supplied by
college workers --- carry higher prices than routine tasks supplied by
non-college workers.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{AI Shock and the Automation Cutoff}
\label{sec:cutoff}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The arrival of AI introduces a competing technology for each task.  In a
competitive market, the price of task $i$ post-AI is driven down to the
minimum of the pre-AI price and the AI cost:
\begin{equation}
  p(i) = \min\!\bigl(p_0(i),\;\tau e^{\alpha i}\bigr).
  \label{eq:taskprice_postai}
\end{equation}
Since $p_0(i)$ is increasing and $\tau e^{\alpha i}$ is also increasing but
at a different rate, under mild regularity conditions there exists a unique
cutoff $I^*$ at which AI cost equals the pre-AI task price:
\begin{equation}
  \tau\, e^{\alpha I^*} = p_0(I^*).
  \label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
For $i < I^*$, AI undercuts the pre-AI price: $p(i) = \tau e^{\alpha i} <
p_0(i)$.  For $i \geq I^*$, the pre-AI price prevails: $p(i) = p_0(i)$.  As
AI improves ($\tau$ falls), the cutoff $I^*$ rises --- the automation frontier
expands toward more complex tasks (see Appendix~\ref{app:cutoff}).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Workers as Task Bundles and Wage Determination}
\label{sec:bundles}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Following \citet{autor2025expertise}, each worker $j$ carries a fixed bundle
$\mathcal{B}_j \subseteq [0,1]$ of tasks that must be performed as an
indivisible unit.  Workers cannot selectively drop AI-exposed tasks while
retaining the rest.

A worker's labor income equals the sum of competitive task prices across their
bundle:
\begin{equation}
  w_j = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j} p(i)\,di.
  \label{eq:bundlewage}
\end{equation}
Pre-AI: $w_j^0 = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j} p_0(i)\,di$.  Post-AI, tasks
$i \in \mathcal{B}_j \cap [0,I^*)$ are repriced below their pre-AI levels, so
the change in income is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta w_j = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j\cap[0,I^*)}
      \underbrace{\bigl(\tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0(i)\bigr)}_{ <\;0 }\,di
  \;\leq\; 0.
  \label{eq:deltaw}
\end{equation}
The sign is unambiguous: by definition of the cutoff, $\tau e^{\alpha i} <
p_0(i)$ for all $i < I^*$, so every repriced task contributes a strictly
negative term.  Wage compression is larger for workers whose bundles overlap
more with the repriced range $[0, I^*)$.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Education Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:het}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\begin{assumption}[Education Sorting]
 WLOG, Non-college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{NC}\subseteq[0,\bar\imath]$
  and college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{C}\subseteq[\bar\imath,1]$,
  with $\bar\imath > I^*$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Differential Wage Compression]
  \label{prop:wages}
  Following a fall in $\tau$ that raises $I^*$ into $(0,\bar\imath)$,
  non-college workers experience strictly larger wage compression than college
  workers: $|\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C| = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent The proof is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.  Since $I^* <
\bar\imath$, the repriced range $[0,I^*)$ lies entirely within the non-college
task domain.  College bundles in $[\bar\imath,1]$ are unaffected by task
repricing and $\Delta w_C = 0$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Unemployment.}  Wage compression is the model's primary
prediction.  Unemployment emerges as its extensive-margin consequence: workers
facing a sufficiently large fall in $w_j$ may exit employment.  We do not
specify the threshold mechanism --- any such specification yields the same
qualitative prediction --- and instead treat unemployment as an empirical
question tested in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
Proposition~\ref{prop:wages} implies that non-college workers are
disproportionately likely to exit.

\begin{remark}
  The near-zero pre-2023 coefficients in the empirical results are consistent
  with $\tau$ remaining stable before the GPT-4 deployment.  The sharp rise in
  effects from 2023 onward corresponds to a discrete fall in $\tau$ that
  pushes $I^*$ into the non-college task range.
\end{remark}

\section{Derivation of the Pre-AI Task Price}
\label{app:foc}

Competitive firms minimize $\int_0^1 p(i)\,y(i)\,di$ subject to
\eqref{eq:ces}.  The first-order condition for task $i$ is:
\[
  p(i) = \mu \cdot \frac{\partial Y}{\partial y(i)}
        = \mu \cdot Y^{1/\sigma} \cdot y(i)^{-1/\sigma},
\]
where $\mu$ is the Lagrange multiplier on the output constraint.  In
competitive equilibrium, $\mu = P_Y$ (the market value of one unit of output),
giving:
\[
  p_0(i) = P_Y \cdot \left(\frac{Y}{y_0(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
\]
The standard CES demand function follows by inverting:
$y_0(i) = Y\cdot(P_Y / p_0(i))^\sigma$. $\blacksquare$

\section{Existence and Comparative Static of the Cutoff}
\label{app:cutoff}

Define $\phi(i) \equiv \tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0(i)$.  At $i=0$, $\phi(0) =
\tau - p_0(0)$.  For a non-trivial cutoff to exist, we require $\phi(0) < 0$
(AI is cheaper than humans at the simplest task) and $\phi(1) > 0$ (humans
retain an advantage at the most complex task).  Under these boundary
conditions and continuity, by the intermediate value theorem there exists
$I^* \in (0,1)$ such that $\phi(I^*)=0$.  Uniqueness holds if
$\phi'(i) = \alpha\tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0'(i) > 0$ at $I^*$, i.e.\ AI costs
rise faster than pre-AI task prices at the cutoff --- a mild condition given
the exponential form of $t(i)$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Comparative static.}  Totally differentiating
$\tau e^{\alpha I^*} = p_0(I^*)$ with respect to $\tau$:
\[
  e^{\alpha I^*} + \tau\alpha e^{\alpha I^*}\frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = p_0'(I^*)\frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}.
\]
Solving:
\[
  \frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = \frac{-e^{\alpha I^*}}{p_0'(I^*) - \alpha\tau e^{\alpha I^*}}
  = \frac{-e^{\alpha I^*}}{p_0'(I^*) - \alpha\, p_0(I^*)}.
\]
This is negative whenever $p_0'(I^*) < \alpha\, p_0(I^*)$, i.e.\ the
percentage rate of increase of pre-AI task prices is less than $\alpha$ at
the cutoff.  Under the exponential parameterization $t(i)=e^{\alpha i}$ this
is the same condition that ensures uniqueness above.  Hence a fall in $\tau$
raises $I^*$. $\blacksquare$

\section{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:wages}}
\label{app:proof}

Under Assumption~3, $\mathcal{B}_{NC}\subseteq[0,\bar\imath]$ and
$\mathcal{B}_C\subseteq[\bar\imath,1]$.  Since $I^*<\bar\imath$, the repriced
range $[0,I^*)$ lies entirely within the non-college task domain.

From \eqref{eq:deltaw}:
\[
  \Delta w_{NC}
  = \int_{\mathcal{B}_{NC}\cap[0,I^*)}
      \bigl(\tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0(i)\bigr)\,di < 0,
\]
since $\tau e^{\alpha i} < p_0(i)$ for all $i < I^*$ by definition of the
cutoff \eqref{eq:cutoff}.

For college workers, $\mathcal{B}_C\cap[0,I^*)=\emptyset$, so $\Delta w_C=0$.

Therefore $|\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C| = 0$. $\blacksquare$
