%==================================================
\section*{From Tasks to Occupations}
%==================================================

The model above treats each worker as a task specialist and delivers a
prediction at the task level: workers whose task lies deep inside
$[0, I^*)$ face wage compression, while workers at or above the
capability frontier gain from output expansion.  The empirical analysis,
however, observes workers at the occupation level.  To bridge the two, I
close the model with a brief accounting exercise that nests
full specialization inside a representative-occupation aggregation.

\paragraph{Occupations as task bundles.}
A representative occupation $x$ is characterized by a fixed bundle density
\[
  a_x : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}_+, \qquad \int_0^1 a_x(i)\, di = 1,
\]
where $a_x(i)$ is the share of working time that a worker in occupation
$x$ devotes to task $i$.  I treat $a_x$ as exogenous throughout --- a
short-run assumption consistent with the construction of the empirical
exposure measure, which uses fixed O*NET task weights.  The task-level
wage schedules $p_0(i)$ and $p_1(i)$ derived above continue to clear the
task market; the occupation layer is purely an aggregation on top.

\paragraph{Wages of a representative occupation.}
A worker in occupation $x$ produces $a_x(i)$ units of task-$i$ output
per unit time and earns the prevailing task price $p(i)$ on each unit.
Pre- and post-AI wages are
\begin{equation}
  w_0(x) = \int_0^1 a_x(i)\, p_0(i)\, di,
  \qquad
  w_1(x) = \int_0^1 a_x(i)\, p_1(i)\, di.
  \label{eq:occ_wages}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Occupation-level wage ratio.}
Let $\omega_x(i) \equiv a_x(i)\, p_0(i) / w_0(x)$ denote the pre-AI
revenue share of task $i$ in occupation $x$.  Combining
\eqref{eq:occ_wages} with the task-level decomposition $p_1/p_0 = (Y_1/Y_0)^{1/\sigma} \cdot (f/(f+s))^{1/\sigma}$ yields
\begin{equation}
  \frac{w_1(x)}{w_0(x)}
  \;=\; \left(\frac{Y_1}{Y_0}\right)^{1/\sigma}
  \cdot \int_0^1 \omega_x(i) \left(\frac{f(i)}{f(i)+s(i)}\right)^{1/\sigma} di.
  \label{eq:occ_ratio}
\end{equation}
The first factor is the common output-expansion boost.  The second
factor is an occupation-specific dilution discount: tasks above $I^*$
contribute $\omega_x(i)$ to the integrand, while tasks below $I^*$
contribute strictly less.

\paragraph{Three-group prediction at the occupation level.}
Equation~\eqref{eq:occ_ratio} inherits the same structure as the
task-level prediction in the workhorse model, now weighted across the
bundle.  Occupations with $a_x$ concentrated above $I^*$ see pure gains;
occupations with $a_x$ heavily weighted toward tasks where AI supply is
large relative to human supply face wage compression; occupations
somewhere in between can still gain if the output boost dominates the
weighted dilution.

\paragraph{Connection to the empirical exposure measure.}
The Eloundou et al.\ beta score approximates
\[
  \beta_x \;=\; \int_0^{I^*} a_x(i)\, di,
\]
the time share of occupation $x$'s bundle devoted to tasks below the
capability frontier.  This is a coarser object than the weighted
dilution integral in~\eqref{eq:occ_ratio}: it uses time shares rather
than revenue shares, and it treats every exposed task symmetrically
rather than weighting by the intensity of AI supply.  Still, $\beta_x$
and the model-implied dilution are ordinally correlated under weak
conditions, which justifies using $\beta_x$ as the occupation-level
regressor in the empirical analysis.  The point of~\eqref{eq:occ_ratio}
is not to propose an alternative regressor but to make explicit how
the task-level mechanism propagates to the occupation-level outcomes
that the data actually records.

\paragraph{Remark: bundle composition as a source of heterogeneity.}
Two occupations with identical $\beta_x$ can have very different
post-AI wage changes if their exposed tasks sit at different points
within $[0, I^*)$.  An occupation whose exposed tasks cluster near
$I^*$ (where $s(i)$ is small) experiences little dilution; an
occupation whose exposed tasks lie deep in $[0, I^*)$ (where $s(i)$ is
large) experiences heavy dilution.  This observation motivates the
heterogeneity analysis in the following sections: apparent
heterogeneity by education or other worker characteristics may be
driven, in part, by systematic differences in bundle composition
across occupations at the same level of measured exposure.
