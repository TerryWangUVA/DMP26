\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Consider a small open economy in which production requires a continuum of
tasks indexed $i \in [0,1]$, ordered by their susceptibility to AI
automation.  Tasks at the low end are easily performed by AI; tasks at the
high end require human judgment, communication, and contextual reasoning that
AI currently struggles to replicate.  This ordering maps directly to the
\citet{eloundou2024gpts} exposure scores: a high exposure score indicates that
a large fraction of an occupation's task bundle is susceptible to large
language models, placing that bundle near the low end of the $i$ spectrum.

Final output $Y$ is produced by assembling tasks via a CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ denotes the quantity of task $i$ and $\sigma$ is the elasticity
of substitution across tasks.

Each task can be performed either by a human worker or by AI.  The AI cost of
performing task $i$ is
\begin{equation}
  c^{\mathrm{AI}}(i) = \tau\cdot t(i),
  \label{eq:ai_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is an aggregate AI capability parameter---lower $\tau$
reflects a more capable and cost-efficient system---and $t(i)$ captures
task-specific automation difficulty, with $t'(i)>0$.  We parameterize
\begin{equation}
  t(i) = e^{\alpha i}, \qquad \alpha > 0,
  \label{eq:cost_fn}
\end{equation}
so that automation costs rise exponentially with task complexity.

\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The economy is a price-taker in the world market for output $Y$.  The output
  price $P_Y$ is exogenous.
\end{assumption}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{From Exogenous Output Price to Equilibrium Task Prices}
\label{sec:taskprices}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

With an exogenous output price $P_Y$, competitive firms take $P_Y$ as given
and minimize the cost of assembling tasks.  Free entry drives profits to zero,
linking $P_Y$ to the cost of producing $Y$:
\begin{equation}
  P_Y = \left[\int_0^1 p(i)^{1-\sigma}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{1}{1-\sigma}},
  \label{eq:zeroprofit}
\end{equation}
where $p(i)$ is the competitive market price of task $i$.

In a competitive task market, each task is supplied by the least-cost
technology.  Let $w$ denote the unit cost of human labor (common across
tasks).  The market price of task $i$ is therefore
\begin{equation}
  p(i) = \min\!\bigl(w,\;\tau t(i)\bigr).
  \label{eq:taskprice}
\end{equation}
This yields a unique cutoff $I^*$ at which human labor and AI are equally
costly:
\begin{equation}
  \tau\, e^{\alpha I^*} = w
  \implies
  I^* = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{w}{\tau}\right).
  \label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
For $i < I^*$, AI is the cheaper technology and $p(i) = \tau e^{\alpha i}$;
for $i \geq I^*$, human labor is cheaper and $p(i) = w$.

Substituting \eqref{eq:taskprice} into the zero-profit condition
\eqref{eq:zeroprofit} yields one equation in one unknown, $w$, given the
exogenous parameters $(P_Y, \tau, \alpha, \sigma)$:
\begin{equation}
  P_Y = \left[
    \int_0^{I^*(w)} \!\bigl(\tau e^{\alpha i}\bigr)^{1-\sigma}di
    \;+\;
    \bigl(1 - I^*(w)\bigr)\, w^{1-\sigma}
  \right]^{\!\frac{1}{1-\sigma}}.
  \label{eq:wagedet}
\end{equation}
This implicitly determines $w = w(P_Y,\tau)$: the human wage is
\emph{endogenous}, pinned by the requirement that competitive firms break even
at the world output price.  The key channel: $P_Y$ is held fixed by world
markets, competitive task pricing drives $p(i)$ to its minimum, and $w$
adjusts so that the CES price index equals $P_Y$.  A derivation and the
comparative static $\partial w/\partial\tau$ are provided in
Appendix~\ref{app:wage}.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Workers as Task Bundles}
\label{sec:bundles}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Rather than assuming workers can freely specialize in any individual task, we
follow the task-bundling insight of \citet{autor2025expertise}: each worker
$j$ is endowed with a fixed bundle $\mathcal{B}_j \subseteq [0,1]$ of tasks
that they perform as an indivisible unit.  Workers cannot selectively drop
AI-exposed tasks from their bundle while retaining the rest.

A worker's labor income equals the sum of competitive market prices of all
tasks in their bundle:
\begin{equation}
  w_j = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j} p(i)\,di
      = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j \cap [0,I^*)} \tau e^{\alpha i}\,di
      \;+\;
        \int_{\mathcal{B}_j \cap [I^*,1]} w\,di.
  \label{eq:bundlewage}
\end{equation}
The economic interpretation is straightforward: because AI can perform tasks
$i < I^*$ at cost $\tau t(i)$, competition caps the market price of those
tasks at $\tau t(i)$, regardless of whether a human performs them.  Workers
whose bundles overlap substantially with $[0,I^*)$ are therefore paid less
for those tasks, compressing their total income.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The AI Shock and Wage Compression}
\label{sec:shock}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Consider a discrete fall in $\tau$ from $\tau_0$ to $\tau_1 < \tau_0$,
representing the deployment of a significantly more capable AI system (e.g.\
GPT-4-class models in 2023).  This shock affects worker income through two
channels.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Task repricing.}  The cutoff rises from
$I^*_0 = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln(w_0/\tau_0)$ to $I^*_1 > I^*_0$.  Tasks in
$[I^*_0, I^*_1]$ transition from human-priced ($p = w_0$) to AI-priced
($p = \tau_1 e^{\alpha i} < w_0$).  For any worker $j$ with
$\mathcal{B}_j \cap [I^*_0, I^*_1] \neq \emptyset$, this directly reduces
$w_j$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Human wage adjustment.}  The fall in $\tau$ also shifts the
zero-profit locus \eqref{eq:wagedet}, changing the equilibrium $w$.  Whether
$w$ rises or falls depends on the relative strength of the displacement effect
(AI absorbs tasks, reducing demand for human labor) and the productivity
effect (cheaper AI lowers $P_Y$'s cost basis, raising demand).  We treat the
net direction as an empirical question; the evidence in
Section~\ref{sec:results} indicates the displacement effect dominates,
consistent with falling wages for high-exposure workers.

\medskip
The total change in worker $j$'s income following the shock is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta w_j
  = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j \cap [I^*_0, I^*_1]}
      \!\bigl(\tau_1 e^{\alpha i} - w_0\bigr)\,di
  \;+\; \Delta w \cdot \bigl|\mathcal{B}_j \cap [I^*_1, 1]\bigr|,
  \label{eq:deltaw}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta w = w_1 - w_0$.  The first term (task repricing) is
unambiguously negative; the second term (human wage adjustment) has ambiguous
sign.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Education Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:model_het}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

To generate testable predictions across education groups, we sort workers by
their bundle composition.  Consistent with the descriptive evidence in
\citet{eloundou2024gpts} and \citet{webb2020impact}, non-college workers hold
bundles concentrated at the low end of the task spectrum---precisely the range
most susceptible to AI repricing.

\begin{assumption}[Education Sorting]
  Non-college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{NC} \subseteq [0,\bar\imath]$
  and college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{C} \subseteq [\bar\imath, 1]$,
  where $\bar\imath \in (I^*_0, 1)$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Differential Wage Compression]
  \label{prop:wages}
  Under Assumption~2, following a fall in $\tau$ that raises $I^*$ into
  $(I^*_0, \bar\imath)$, non-college workers experience strictly larger wage
  compression than college workers:
  \[
    |\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C|.
  \]
\end{proposition}

\noindent The proof is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_wage}.  The intuition is
immediate from \eqref{eq:deltaw}: non-college bundles overlap substantially
with $[I^*_0, I^*_1]$ (the repriced range), while college bundles in
$[\bar\imath, 1]$ lie above this range and are unaffected by task repricing.
College workers are only exposed through the aggregate wage adjustment
$\Delta w$, which is a common term across all workers.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Unemployment.}  The model delivers a clean prediction about
wage compression.  Unemployment is a natural extensive-margin consequence: a
worker facing a sufficiently large $\Delta w_j$ may exit employment rather
than supply labor at the compressed wage.  We do not specify the threshold
mechanism---any such mechanism (wage floor, outside option, fixed cost of
employment) yields the same qualitative prediction---and instead treat
unemployment as an empirical question tested directly in
Section~\ref{sec:results}.  Proposition~\ref{prop:wages} implies that, under
any such mechanism, non-college workers are disproportionately likely to exit.

\begin{remark}
  The model's prediction that the AI shock's labor market effects are
  concentrated post-2023 follows from the discrete nature of the $\tau$ shock.
  The pre-trends (near-zero interaction effects in 2018--2022) are consistent
  with no meaningful change in $\tau$ during that period, while the sharp rise
  in effects from 2023 onward aligns with the deployment of GPT-4-class
  systems.
\end{remark}

\section{Wage Determination: Derivation and Comparative Static}
\label{app:wage}

\noindent\textbf{Derivation.}  Substituting $p(i) = \min(w, \tau e^{\alpha i})$
and $I^* = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln(w/\tau)$ into the zero-profit condition
\eqref{eq:zeroprofit}:
\begin{align*}
  P_Y^{1-\sigma}
  &= \int_0^{I^*} \!\tau^{1-\sigma} e^{\alpha(1-\sigma)i}\,di
   + \int_{I^*}^{1} w^{1-\sigma}\,di \\
  &= \frac{\tau^{1-\sigma}}{\alpha(1-\sigma)}
     \Bigl[e^{\alpha(1-\sigma)I^*} - 1\Bigr]
   + (1-I^*)\,w^{1-\sigma}.
\end{align*}
Since $I^* = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln(w/\tau)$, we have $e^{\alpha I^*} = w/\tau$,
so $e^{\alpha(1-\sigma)I^*} = (w/\tau)^{1-\sigma}$.  Substituting:
\[
  P_Y^{1-\sigma}
  = \frac{w^{1-\sigma} - \tau^{1-\sigma}}{\alpha(1-\sigma)}
  + \left(1 - \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\frac{w}{\tau}\right)w^{1-\sigma}.
\]
This is one equation in $w$ given $(P_Y, \tau, \alpha, \sigma)$, which
implicitly defines $w = w(P_Y, \tau)$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Comparative static.}  Totally differentiating
\eqref{eq:wagedet} with respect to $\tau$ (holding $P_Y$ fixed) yields
$\partial w/\partial\tau$, whose sign depends on $\sigma$.  For $\sigma > 1$
(tasks are substitutes), a fall in $\tau$ raises demand for human tasks and
$\partial w/\partial\tau < 0$ (wages rise with AI improvement).  For
$\sigma < 1$ (tasks are complements), $\partial w/\partial\tau > 0$ (wages
fall).  The empirical evidence in Section~\ref{sec:results} is consistent with
the $\sigma < 1$ case, or with the displacement effect dominating at the
margins studied.

\section{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:wages}}
\label{app:proof_wage}

Under Assumption~2, $\mathcal{B}_{NC} \subseteq [0,\bar\imath]$ and
$\mathcal{B}_{C} \subseteq [\bar\imath,1]$.  Since $I^*_1 < \bar\imath$
(the shock is moderate), the repriced range $[I^*_0, I^*_1]$ lies entirely
within the non-college task range.

From \eqref{eq:deltaw}, the task-repricing component of $\Delta w_j$ is:
\[
  \Delta w_j^{\mathrm{reprice}}
  = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j \cap [I^*_0,\,I^*_1]}
    \bigl(\tau_1 e^{\alpha i} - w_0\bigr)\,di.
\]
Since $\tau_1 e^{\alpha i} < w_0$ for all $i \in [I^*_0, I^*_1]$, each
integrand is strictly negative.

For non-college workers: $\mathcal{B}_{NC} \cap [I^*_0, I^*_1] \neq
\emptyset$, so $\Delta w_{NC}^{\mathrm{reprice}} < 0$.

For college workers: $\mathcal{B}_C \subseteq [\bar\imath, 1]$ and
$I^*_1 < \bar\imath$, so $\mathcal{B}_C \cap [I^*_0, I^*_1] = \emptyset$
and $\Delta w_C^{\mathrm{reprice}} = 0$.

Both groups share the same aggregate wage adjustment $\Delta w$ (second term
in \eqref{eq:deltaw}).  Therefore $|\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C|$.
$\blacksquare$
