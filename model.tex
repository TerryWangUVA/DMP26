\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Consider a small open economy in which production requires a continuum of
\emph{cognitive tasks} indexed $i \in [0,1]$, ordered by their susceptibility
to AI automation.  We restrict attention to cognitive tasks because large
language models operate primarily through language and reasoning. Tasks at the low
end of $i$ are routine cognitive tasks easily replicated by AI; tasks at the
high end are non-routine cognitive tasks that require human expertise that AI
currently struggles to replicate.  A high \citet{eloundou2024gpts} exposure
score places an occupation's tasks near the low end of the $i$ spectrum.

Final output $Y$ aggregates tasks via a CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ is the quantity of task $i$ used in production.  Each task can
additionally be performed by AI at cost
\begin{equation}
  c^{\mathrm{AI}}(i) = \tau\cdot t(i), \qquad t(i) = e^{\alpha i},\quad\alpha>0,
  \label{eq:ai_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\tau>0$ is an aggregate AI capability parameter (lower $\tau$ reflects
more capable AI) and $t'(i)>0$ ensures that more complex tasks are costlier
to automate.

\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The economy is a price-taker in the world market for $Y$.  The output price
  $P_Y$ is exogenous and fixed.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Full Specialization]
  Workers are indexed by ability type $i^* \in [0,1]$.  A worker of type
  $i^*$ is capable of performing any task $i \leq i^*$ but fully specializes
  in task $i^*$ --- the hardest task they can perform, at which they hold
  comparative advantage over other types.  Each task $i$ is therefore supplied exclusively by
  workers of type $i^*=i$.
\end{assumption}

\noindent Full specialization is the natural competitive assignment: workers
sort to the task at which their relative productivity is highest, analogous
to comparative advantage in trade models.  It eliminates the need to treat
workers as indivisible bundles of tasks while preserving the key
heterogeneity --- workers differ in their susceptibility to AI automation.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Pre-AI Task Prices and Wages}
\label{sec:taskprices}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Before AI, all tasks are performed by human workers.  Competitive firms
minimize cost subject to \eqref{eq:ces}, taking $P_Y$ as given.  The
first-order condition for task $i$ yields the equilibrium task price
(derived in Appendix~\ref{app:foc}):
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = P_Y \cdot \left(\frac{Y}{y_0(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma},
  \label{eq:taskprice_preai}
\end{equation}
where $y_0(i)$ is the pre-AI equilibrium quantity of task $i$.  Under full
specialization, $y_0(i) = f(i)$, where $f$ is the density of worker types:
task supply equals the mass of workers specializing in $i$.

Under full specialization, a worker of type $i^*$ earns the competitive
price of their task:
\begin{equation}
  w_0(i^*) = p_0(i^*).
  \label{eq:wage_preai}
\end{equation}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{AI Shock and the Cutoff}
\label{sec:cutoff}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The arrival of AI introduces a competing technology for each task.  In a
competitive market, the price of task $i$ post-AI is driven down to the
minimum of the pre-AI price and the AI cost:
\begin{equation}
  p(i) = \min\!\bigl(p_0(i),\;\tau e^{\alpha i}\bigr).
  \label{eq:taskprice_postai}
\end{equation}
Under mild regularity conditions there exists a unique cutoff $I^*$ at which
AI cost equals the pre-AI task price:
\begin{equation}
  \tau\, e^{\alpha I^*} = p_0(I^*).
  \label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
For $i < I^*$, AI undercuts the pre-AI price: $p(i) = \tau e^{\alpha i} <
p_0(i)$.  For $i \geq I^*$, the pre-AI price prevails: $p(i) = p_0(i)$.  As
AI improves ($\tau$ falls), the cutoff $I^*$ rises --- the automation frontier
expands toward more complex tasks (see Appendix~\ref{app:cutoff}).

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Wages Post-AI}
\label{sec:wages}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

A worker of type $i^*$ earns the post-AI competitive price of their task:
$w(i^*) = p(i^*)$.  The change in wages following the AI shock is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta w(i^*) \;=\; p(i^*) - p_0(i^*) \;=\;
  \begin{cases}
    \tau e^{\alpha i^*} - p_0(i^*) < 0 & \text{if } i^* < I^*, \\[4pt]
    0 & \text{if } i^* \geq I^*.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:deltaw}
\end{equation}
For workers below the cutoff, the task price is driven down to the AI cost.
By definition of $I^*$, $\tau e^{\alpha i} < p_0(i)$ for all $i < I^*$, so
$\Delta w(i^*) < 0$ is unambiguous.  Workers above the cutoff are unaffected
in partial equilibrium.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Displacement.}  If a worker's compressed wage
$\tau e^{\alpha i^*}$ falls below a reservation wage $\underline{w}$ --- any
threshold reflecting outside options, search frictions, or a wage floor ---
the worker exits employment.  The sign is the same under any such
specification; we do not model the threshold explicitly and instead treat
unemployment as an empirical question tested in Section~\ref{sec:results}.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Productivity effect.}  Automating low-$i$ tasks lowers the
cost of producing $Y$, which in a general equilibrium setting would raise
output and potentially increase wages for workers at $i^* \geq I^*$.  We
abstract from this channel --- consistent with the PE, small open economy
framework --- and note it as a caveat: our model provides a lower bound on
wages for retained workers.

\section{Derivation of the Pre-AI Task Price}
\label{app:foc}

There are two actors in the task market: firms on the demand side and workers
on the supply side.  We derive equilibrium task prices in two stages.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 1: Firm's demand for tasks.}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 1.}
A competitive firm takes the output price $P_Y$ and all task prices
$\{p_0(i)\}$ as given.  It chooses task quantities $\{y(i)\}$ to minimize
cost subject to the CES technology \eqref{eq:ces}:
\[
  \min_{\{y(i)\}} \int_0^1 p_0(i)\,y(i)\,di
  \quad\text{s.t.}\quad
  \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}}
  \geq \bar Y.
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 2.}
Let $\mu$ be the Lagrange multiplier on the output constraint.  The
first-order condition for task $i$ is:
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = \mu\cdot\frac{\partial Y}{\partial y(i)}.
  \label{eq:foc_general}
\end{equation}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 3.}
Write $Y = Z^{\sigma/(\sigma-1)}$ where $Z \equiv \int_0^1
y(j)^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}\,dj$.  By the chain rule:
\[
  \frac{\partial Y}{\partial y(i)}
  = \frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}\,Z^{\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}-1}
    \cdot \frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}\,y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}-1}
  = Z^{\frac{1}{\sigma-1}}\cdot y(i)^{-\frac{1}{\sigma}}.
\]
Since $Z = Y^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}$, it follows that $Z^{1/(\sigma-1)} =
Y^{1/\sigma}$.  Substituting:
\[
  \frac{\partial Y}{\partial y(i)}
  = \left(\frac{Y}{y(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
\]

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 4.}
In competitive equilibrium the multiplier equals the output price, $\mu =
P_Y$.  Substituting into \eqref{eq:foc_general} and rearranging gives the
firm's demand for task $i$:
\begin{equation}
  y^{d}(i) = Y\cdot\left(\frac{P_Y}{p_0(i)}\right)^{\!\sigma}.
  \label{eq:task_demand}
\end{equation}
Demand is decreasing in the task's own price and increasing in aggregate
output.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Stage 2: Market clearing and equilibrium price.}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 5.}
Let $y_0(i)$ denote the pre-AI supply of task $i$ --- the quantity supplied
by human workers before the AI shock.  Under full specialization
(Assumption~2), $y_0(i) = f(i)$, the density of workers of type $i^*=i$.
Setting demand equal to supply:
\[
  y^{d}(i) = y_0(i)
  \implies
  Y\cdot\left(\frac{P_Y}{p_0(i)}\right)^{\!\sigma} = y_0(i).
\]
Solving for the equilibrium task price:
\begin{equation*}
  p_0(i) = P_Y\cdot\left(\frac{Y}{y_0(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
\end{equation*}
$\blacksquare$

\section{Existence and Comparative Static of the Cutoff}
\label{app:cutoff}

Define $\phi(i) \equiv \tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0(i)$.  At $i=0$, $\phi(0) =
\tau - p_0(0)$.  For a non-trivial cutoff to exist, we require $\phi(0) < 0$
(AI is cheaper than humans at the simplest task) and $\phi(1) > 0$ (humans
retain an advantage at the most complex task).  Under these boundary
conditions and continuity, by the intermediate value theorem there exists
$I^* \in (0,1)$ such that $\phi(I^*)=0$.  Uniqueness holds if
$\phi'(i) = \alpha\tau e^{\alpha i} - p_0'(i) > 0$ at $I^*$, i.e.\ AI costs
rise faster than pre-AI task prices at the cutoff --- an acceptable condition given
the exponential form of $t(i)$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Comparative static.}  Totally differentiating
$\tau e^{\alpha I^*} = p_0(I^*)$ with respect to $\tau$:
\[
  e^{\alpha I^*} + \tau\alpha e^{\alpha I^*}\frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = p_0'(I^*)\frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}.
\]
Solving:
\[
  \frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = \frac{-e^{\alpha I^*}}{p_0'(I^*) - \alpha\tau e^{\alpha I^*}}
  = \frac{-e^{\alpha I^*}}{p_0'(I^*) - \alpha\, p_0(I^*)}.
\]
This is negative whenever $p_0'(I^*) < \alpha\, p_0(I^*)$, i.e.\ the
percentage rate of increase of pre-AI task prices is less than $\alpha$ at
the cutoff.  Under the exponential parameterization $t(i)=e^{\alpha i}$ this
is the same condition that ensures uniqueness above.  Hence a fall in $\tau$
raises $I^*$. $\blacksquare$
