\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}
% =============================================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Consider a small open economy in which production requires a continuum of
tasks indexed $i \in [0,1]$, ordered by their susceptibility to AI
automation.  Tasks at the low end are easily performed by AI; tasks at the
high end require human judgment and contextual reasoning that AI currently
struggles to replicate.  This ordering maps directly to the
\citet{eloundou2024gpts} exposure scores: a high exposure score indicates that
a large fraction of an occupation's task bundle is susceptible to large
language models, placing that bundle near the low end of the $i$ spectrum.

Final output $Y$ aggregates tasks via a symmetric CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ is the quantity of task $i$ and $\sigma$ is the elasticity of
substitution across tasks.  Each task $i$ can additionally be performed by AI
at cost
\begin{equation}
  c^{\mathrm{AI}}(i) = \tau\cdot t(i), \qquad t(i) = e^{\alpha i},\quad\alpha>0,
  \label{eq:ai_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\tau>0$ is an aggregate AI capability parameter (lower $\tau$ reflects
more capable AI) and $t'(i)>0$ ensures that more complex tasks are costlier
to automate.

\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The economy is a price-taker in the world market for $Y$.  The output price
  $P_Y$ is exogenous and fixed.
\end{assumption}

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Pre-AI Task Prices and the AI Shock}
\label{sec:taskprices}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

\noindent\textbf{Pre-AI equilibrium.}
Before AI, all tasks are performed by human workers.  We normalize the unit
of measurement for each task $i$ so that one unit of task $i$ costs $P_Y$ in
the pre-AI world:
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = P_Y \quad \text{for all } i \in [0,1].
  \label{eq:norm}
\end{equation}
This is a units convention, not a derived result: with heterogeneous worker
bundles, tasks in different ranges of $i$ are supplied by different groups
(non-college vs.\ college) and need not be equally scarce.  The normalization
provides a clean benchmark against which the AI shock's effect on task prices
can be measured.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{AI shock.}
The arrival of AI (parameterized by a fall in $\tau$) introduces a competing
technology for each task.  In a competitive task market, the price of task $i$
is driven down to the minimum of the pre-AI human price and the AI cost:
\begin{equation}
  p(i) = \min\!\bigl(P_Y,\;\tau e^{\alpha i}\bigr).
  \label{eq:taskprice}
\end{equation}
This yields a unique automation cutoff $I^*$ at which AI cost equals the
pre-AI task price:
\begin{equation}
  \tau\, e^{\alpha I^*} = P_Y
  \quad\Longrightarrow\quad
  I^* = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{P_Y}{\tau}\right).
  \label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
For $i < I^*$, AI undercuts the pre-AI price and $p(i) = \tau e^{\alpha i} <
P_Y$; for $i \geq I^*$, the pre-AI price holds and $p(i) = P_Y$.  As AI
improves ($\tau$ falls), $I^*$ rises and more tasks are repriced downward:
\begin{equation}
  \frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau} = -\frac{1}{\alpha\tau} < 0.
  \label{eq:cs}
\end{equation}
Crucially, the cutoff $I^*$ is determined entirely by the two exogenous
parameters $P_Y$ and $\tau$ — no additional equilibrium condition is required.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Workers as Task Bundles and Wage Determination}
\label{sec:bundles}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Following \citet{autor2025expertise}, we treat each worker $j$ as carrying a
fixed bundle $\mathcal{B}_j \subseteq [0,1]$ of tasks that must be performed
as an indivisible unit.  Workers cannot selectively drop AI-exposed tasks from
their bundle while retaining the rest.

A worker's labor income is the sum of competitive market prices across their
bundle:
\begin{equation}
  w_j = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j} p(i)\,di.
  \label{eq:bundlewage}
\end{equation}
Pre-AI, $w_j^0 = P_Y\cdot|\mathcal{B}_j|$, where $|\mathcal{B}_j|$ denotes
the measure of the bundle.  Post-AI, tasks $i \in \mathcal{B}_j \cap [0,I^*)$
are repriced below $P_Y$, so:
\begin{equation}
  w_j^1 =
    \int_{\mathcal{B}_j\cap[0,I^*)} \!\tau e^{\alpha i}\,di
    \;+\; P_Y\cdot\bigl|\mathcal{B}_j\cap[I^*,1]\bigr|.
  \label{eq:bundlewage_post}
\end{equation}
The change in income is therefore:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta w_j = w_j^1 - w_j^0
  = \int_{\mathcal{B}_j\cap[0,I^*)}
      \underbrace{\bigl(\tau e^{\alpha i} - P_Y\bigr)}_{<\;0}\,di
  \;\leq\; 0.
  \label{eq:deltaw}
\end{equation}
The sign is unambiguous: every repriced task contributes a strictly negative
term.  Workers whose bundles overlap more with $[0,I^*)$ suffer larger wage
compression.  Note that no separate human wage needs to be determined: because
workers are bundles of tasks rather than suppliers of homogeneous labor, wages
emerge directly from the sum of task prices in the bundle.

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Education Heterogeneity}
\label{sec:het}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Consistent with the descriptive evidence in \citet{eloundou2024gpts} and
\citet{webb2020impact}, non-college workers hold bundles concentrated at the
low end of the task spectrum, while college workers hold bundles concentrated
at the high end.

\begin{assumption}[Education Sorting]
  Non-college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{NC}\subseteq[0,\bar\imath]$
  and college workers hold bundles $\mathcal{B}_{C}\subseteq[\bar\imath,1]$,
  with $\bar\imath\in(I^*,1)$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{proposition}[Differential Wage Compression]
  \label{prop:wages}
  Following a fall in $\tau$ that raises $I^*$ into $(0,\bar\imath)$,
  non-college workers experience strictly larger wage compression than college
  workers: $|\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C| = 0$.
\end{proposition}

\noindent The proof is in Appendix~\ref{app:proof}.  The intuition follows
directly from \eqref{eq:deltaw}: the repriced range $[0,I^*)$ lies entirely
within the non-college task domain $[0,\bar\imath]$, so only non-college
bundles incur the negative repricing term.  College bundles in
$[\bar\imath,1]$ remain above the automation frontier and are unaffected.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Unemployment.}  Wage compression constitutes the model's
primary prediction.  Unemployment emerges naturally as its extensive-margin
consequence: a worker facing a sufficiently large fall in $w_j$ may exit
employment.  We do not specify the threshold mechanism, as any such
specification---wage floor, outside option, or employment fixed cost---yields
the same qualitative prediction.  The unemployment effect is therefore treated
as an empirical question and tested directly in Section~\ref{sec:results}.
Proposition~\ref{prop:wages} implies that, under any threshold mechanism,
non-college workers are disproportionately likely to exit.

\begin{remark}
  The near-zero pre-2023 interaction effects in the empirical results are
  consistent with $\tau$ remaining stable before the GPT-4 deployment.  The
  sharp rise in effects from 2023 onward corresponds to a discrete fall in
  $\tau$, raising $I^*$ into the non-college task range.
\end{remark}

\section{Derivation of the Cutoff and Comparative Static}
\label{app:cutoff}

Post-AI, task $i$ is priced at $p(i) = \min(P_Y,\,\tau e^{\alpha i})$.  AI
undercuts the pre-AI price when $\tau e^{\alpha i} < P_Y$, i.e.\
$i < \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln(P_Y/\tau)$.  The cutoff equates AI cost to the
pre-AI task price:
\[
  \tau e^{\alpha I^*} = P_Y
  \implies
  I^* = \frac{1}{\alpha}\ln\!\left(\frac{P_Y}{\tau}\right).
\]
For $I^*\in(0,1)$ we require $P_Y/e^{\alpha} < \tau < P_Y$, which holds at
the pre-shock parameter values.  Differentiating with respect to $\tau$:
\[
  \frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = \frac{1}{\alpha}\cdot\frac{-1}{\tau}
  = -\frac{1}{\alpha\tau} < 0.
\]
A fall in $\tau$ raises $I^*$: the automation frontier expands toward more
complex tasks. $\blacksquare$

\section{Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:wages}}
\label{app:proof}

Under Assumption~2, $\mathcal{B}_{NC}\subseteq[0,\bar\imath]$ and
$\mathcal{B}_C\subseteq[\bar\imath,1]$.  Since $I^*<\bar\imath$, the repriced
range $[0,I^*)$ lies entirely within the non-college task domain.

From \eqref{eq:deltaw}:
\[
  \Delta w_{NC}
  = \int_{\mathcal{B}_{NC}\cap[0,I^*)}
      \bigl(\tau e^{\alpha i} - P_Y\bigr)\,di < 0,
\]
since $\tau e^{\alpha i} < P_Y$ for all $i < I^*$ by definition of the
cutoff.

For college workers, $\mathcal{B}_C\subseteq[\bar\imath,1]$ and
$[0,I^*)\cap[\bar\imath,1]=\emptyset$, so:
\[
  \Delta w_C = 0.
\]
Therefore $|\Delta w_{NC}| > |\Delta w_C| = 0$. $\blacksquare$
