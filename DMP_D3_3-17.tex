\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{CPS-ASEC}

The primary data source is the Annual Social and Economic Supplement to the Current Population Survey (CPS-ASEC), accessed through IPUMS. The CPS-ASEC is a nationally representative annual survey that captures labor-force status, occupation, wage income, and detailed demographic characteristics for approximately 100,000 households. I use eight survey waves covering income reference years 2018--2025, spanning both the pre- and post-ChatGPT periods.

I restrict the sample to respondents aged 22--65 who are active in the labor force---either employed or actively seeking work. Workers with less than a high school diploma are excluded. After merging with the AI exposure variable and dropping observations with missing values, the main estimation sample contains 500,669 person-year observations.

\paragraph{Unemployment.} The unemployment indicator equals one if a respondent is classified as unemployed by the CPS---not working but actively searching for work (CPS \texttt{empstat} codes 21 and 22)---and zero otherwise. The sample mean is 3.4\%, roughly consistent with official U.S. unemployment statistics over this period.

\paragraph{Wages.} For the wage analysis, I use annual real wage and salary income (\texttt{incwage}), deflated to 1982 dollars using the annual average CPI-U from FRED. The outcome variable is the natural log of real wages. Observations with zero or missing wage income are excluded from the wage sample (workers who are unemployed or out of the labor force for wage purposes), yielding 464,300 observations.

\subsection{AI Exposure}

The AI exposure variable is the GPT-4 annotator beta score (\texttt{dv\_rating\_beta}) from \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}. \textcite{eloundou2024gpts} measure occupation-level exposure to large language models using a task-based annotation methodology. Drawing on the O*NET task inventory, trained human annotators and GPT-4 itself independently assessed each task in each occupation according to whether access to a GPT-4-type system could reduce the time required to complete that task by at least 50\%. Scores are then aggregated to the occupational level as the share of tasks judged highly susceptible to LLM substitution or augmentation.

The study produces three variants of the score corresponding to three assumptions about the technological capability frontier. The \emph{alpha} score (most conservative) assumes access to the bare GPT-4 API only, with no additional software tools. The \emph{gamma} score (most expansive) imagines hypothetical future software that leverages GPT-4 to its full potential. The \emph{beta} score occupies the middle ground: it assumes access to GPT-4 together with plausible near-term software complements---such as code interpreters and retrieval-augmented search---that were realistic at the time of writing. I use beta as the main exposure measure. It strikes a balance between the overly conservative alpha (which ignores the ecosystem of applications already built around LLMs) and the speculative gamma (which conflates current AI with a hypothetical superintelligence). Beta is therefore the most economically interpretable measure for studying the labor-market impact of generative AI as it has actually been deployed.

The variable runs from approximately 0 (low exposure, e.g., Construction and Extraction Occupations) to approximately 0.78 (high exposure, e.g., Computer and Mathematical, Legal, Business and Financial Operations Occupations), with a sample standard deviation of roughly 0.17. Because the scale is bounded within $[0,1]$, regression coefficients are best interpreted per 0.1-unit increase in the beta score rather than per unit---a 1-unit shift would correspond to an implausibly large move from zero to full exposure. All coefficient interpretations in this paper use 0.1-unit increments unless otherwise stated. The variable is time-invariant and assigned at the occupational level.

Because the CPS-ASEC codes occupations using a Census-based scheme distinct from the SOC codes used by \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}, I construct a hierarchical crosswalk: from Census 2010 occupation codes to 6-digit 2010 SOC codes, then from 2010 SOC to 2018 SOC using the BLS official crosswalk. Where exact 6-digit matches are unavailable, exposure scores are aggregated at the 5-digit and 4-digit SOC levels, taking group means. This procedure retains nearly all observations in the sample.

\paragraph{Potential experience.} For the experience heterogeneity specification, I impute potential labor-market experience as $\max(\text{age} - \text{years of schooling} - 6,\, 0)$, with years of schooling assigned from the CPS education recode.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Main Specification}

The core empirical question is whether outcomes for workers in high-exposure occupations moved differently from outcomes in low-exposure occupations after ChatGPT's release, relative to how they moved before. The estimating equation is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $Y_{it}$ is either the unemployment indicator or log wages for worker $i$ in year $t$; $\text{AIExposure}_i$ is the time-invariant exposure score; and year 2022 is the omitted category. The $\beta_t$ coefficients trace, year by year, whether high-exposure workers diverge from low-exposure workers relative to the 2022 baseline. If AI caused labor-market disruption after its diffusion, we expect the post-2022 $\beta_t$ to be positive (for unemployment) or negative (for wages), while the pre-2022 $\beta_t$ should hover near zero.

Three progressively richer specifications are estimated: (1) no additional controls, (2) sex fixed effects, and (3) sex and education fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the occupation level throughout.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: Experience and Education}

\paragraph{Experience.} To examine whether the effect varies by seniority, I augment the main specification with a triple interaction:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:triple}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \delta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{Experience}_i \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it}.
\end{equation}
A negative post-2022 $\delta_t$ would indicate that more-experienced workers are relatively shielded; a positive value would indicate they are disproportionately affected. This specification is estimated separately for the college and non-college subsamples, allowing the experience gradient to differ across education groups.

\paragraph{Education: split-sample approach.} For the education dimension I divide the sample into \emph{college-educated} workers (BA or above, educ $>$ 110) and \emph{non-college} workers (high school or some college, educ $\leq$ 110) and estimate the main specification \eqref{eq:main} separately within each group:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:split}
Y_{it}^{\,g} = \alpha^g
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t^g \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma^g + \varepsilon_{it}^g, \quad g \in \{\text{college},\;\text{non-college}\}.
\end{equation}
This approach is more transparent than a pooled regression with triple interactions: each subsample has its own baseline, trend, and exposure gradient, and the two coefficient paths $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{col}}\}$ and $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{noncol}}\}$ can be compared directly on a combined coefficient plot. The identifying variation within each subsample is unchanged---occupation-level AI exposure interacted with year dummies---so the parallel-trends assumption is assessed separately for each group.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Unemployment}

Table~\ref{tab:unemployment_main} and Figures~\ref{fig:unemployment_naive_plot}--\ref{fig:unemployment_sexeducfe_plot} present the unemployment results. The pattern is clear and remarkably stable across all three specifications.

In the pre-ChatGPT period, coefficients for 2018 and 2019 are statistically indistinguishable from zero---the unemployment paths of high- and low-exposure workers were parallel before AI diffusion, satisfying the key identifying assumption. The pandemic years (2020--2021) present a striking negative: AI-exposed occupations experienced \emph{lower} unemployment during COVID-19, with coefficients of $-0.0213^{***}$ in 2020 and $-0.0335^{***}$ in 2021. This reflects the simple fact that high-exposure occupations---legal, financial, managerial, computational---were largely remote-compatible and thus spared the layoffs concentrated in in-person services.

The post-2022 reversal is the central finding. The 2023 coefficient is small and insignificant ($+0.002$), consistent with a natural lag between model release and meaningful workforce adjustment. By 2024, the coefficient rises to $+0.0206^{***}$ and remains at $+0.0194^{***}$ in 2025. Moving from the lowest- to the highest-exposure occupation (a span of roughly 0.73 units on the exposure scale) is associated with a predicted difference of about 1.5 percentage points in unemployment probability---equivalent to a 44\% increase relative to the 3.4\% baseline. This effect is entirely robust to controlling for sex or education composition across occupations.

\subsection{Wages}

Table~\ref{tab:logwage_main} presents the log-wage regression results. In contrast to the unemployment findings, the wage picture is largely quiet. Most year-by-year coefficients on (year $\times$ AI exposure) are small and statistically insignificant across all three specifications. The one exception is the 2025 coefficient in the most demanding specification (sex and education FE), which is $-0.059^{**}$---a modest but statistically meaningful wage compression in the most recent year. The pre-2022 wage coefficients are also not statistically significant, and no clear post-ChatGPT trend is discernible in wages the way there is in unemployment.

This divergence between large unemployment effects and muted wage effects tells us something about the adjustment mechanism. In a frictionless labor market, a technology that reduces demand for certain tasks would show up as lower wages for affected workers---firms would cut pay before cutting headcount. The data suggest that is not what is happening. Instead, it appears that firms are reducing employment in exposed roles---not hiring, not renewing contracts, letting positions go---without markedly reducing the wages of those who remain. The labor-market adjustment to generative AI, at least so far, is a quantity adjustment rather than a price adjustment.
