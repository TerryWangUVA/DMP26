\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Technology, Tasks, and Labor Markets}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{acemoglu2019automation})}
    Develops a theoretical model explaining how automation both displaces existing tasks and creates new ones.
    Highlights the ambiguous effects of technology on labor demand depending on whether new tasks are complements or substitutes for human labor.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{ottaviano2013immigration})}
    Extends the \textcite{grossman2008trading} task-trade framework to allow heterogeneous worker types and multiple cutoffs, where different skill groups face different automation (offshoring) thresholds.
    Originally developed to study offshoring and immigration — not AI — but the formal structure maps directly onto AI-shock analysis: the cutoff mechanism and comparative statics this paper uses are drawn from this framework.
    Illustrates how a model built in 2013 for a different labor-market shock generalizes to the generative AI context with minimal modification.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{autor2025expertise})}
    Argues that real-world workers are not single-task specialists but hold \emph{bundles} of complementary tasks whose value derives from expertise accumulated over time.
    Formal model shows that task automation within a bundle can both displace and restructure work depending on whether the automated task is central to the bundle's value.
    Provides a more nuanced treatment of worker-task assignment than the full-specialization benchmark used in this paper; to the extent workers hold expertise bundles, the wage compression result here is a lower bound and displacement estimates may understate adjustment costs.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Measuring Exposure to Generative AI}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2018method})}
    Introduces a quantitative approach linking progress in distinct AI subfields (from the EFF AI Progress metrics) to human abilities defined in O*NET.
    Maps AI capabilities $\rightarrow$ O*NET abilities $\rightarrow$ occupations.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2021aioe})}
    Extends the 2018 framework to a forward-looking exposure index using Mechanical Turk surveys to map ten AI applications to 52 O*NET abilities.
    Aggregates ability-level exposure (weighted by importance and prevalence) to occupation, industry, and geographic levels.
    Produces standardized exposure scores such as \textit{AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE)}, \textit{AI Industry Exposure (AIIE)}, and \textit{AI Geography Exposure (AIGE)}, measuring potential technical exposure rather than realized adoption.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{webb2020impact})}
    Constructs a task-technology overlap model matching verbs and nouns from patent text to O*NET task descriptions.
    Identifies high-skill cognitive tasks (judgment, analysis) as most AI-exposed, contrasting with robotics' focus on routine manual work.
    Provides a scalable, data-driven mapping of AI technologies to tasks using textual similarity.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{eloundou2024gpts})}
    Uses GPT-4 and human annotators to classify O*NET tasks into categories based on whether LLMs could reduce task completion time by at least 50\%. Aggregates task labels to occupation-level exposure scores and produces composite indices.
    Provides a large-scale, LLM-specific exposure dataset reflecting technical feasibility rather than observed adoption.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{handa2025economic})}
    Analyzes over four million anonymized Claude.ai conversations, mapping them to O*NET tasks and occupations to produce the first large-scale empirical portrait of how AI is \emph{actually used} across the economy --- approximating real-world adoption rather than technical feasibility.
    Finds that AI use is concentrated in software development and writing; roughly 36\% of occupations use AI for at least a quarter of their tasks; 57\% of interactions suggest augmentation while 43\% suggest automation.
    Represents arguably the most current measure of AI exposure, incorporating observed behavior rather than expert or model annotation. This paper's analysis predates its release and relies on \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}; incorporating usage-based measures is a natural direction for future work.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the Evidence Shows So Far}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{hosseini2025seniority})}
Examines whether generative AI constitutes a seniority-biased technological change, disproportionately affecting junior relative to senior workers.
Combines LinkedIn résumé data (Revelio Labs) with nearly 200 million job postings. Measures firm-level AI adoption by identifying postings for ``GenAI integrator'' roles responsible for implementing or integrating AI tools. Employs a difference-in-differences framework comparing junior versus senior employment in adopting versus non-adopting firms before and after 2023.
Finds that after ChatGPT's release, junior employment in adopting firms declined by roughly 9--10\%, while senior employment remained stable, with effects concentrated in high-exposure occupations.  Provides early empirical evidence that generative AI adoption shifts firm workforce composition toward senior labor.
    \item \textbf{(\cite{brynjolfsson2025canaries})}
Uses high-frequency administrative payroll data from ADP, covering millions of U.S. workers, to document six stylized facts about the early labor market effects of generative AI.
Identifies that employment among early-career workers (ages 22--25) in highly AI-exposed occupations has declined by roughly 13\% since late 2022, even after controlling for firm-level shocks.
Employment for older or less AI-exposed workers remained stable or continued to grow.
Shows that employment declines are concentrated in occupations where AI automates rather than augments work, based on task-level measures from the Anthropic Economic Index \parencite{handa2025economic} and GPT-4 exposure \parencite{eloundou2024gpts}.
Finds limited wage effects, suggesting adjustments occurred primarily through employment rather than compensation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Toy Model}
\label{sec:model}
%==================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Production requires a continuum of cognitive tasks indexed $i \in [0,1]$,
ordered by susceptibility to AI automation. Tasks at the low end are routine cognitive tasks easily replicated by large language models; tasks at the high end require human expertise that AI currently struggles to replicate.


Final output $Y$ aggregates tasks via a CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ is the quantity of task $i$ used in production.

AI can additionally supply a subset of these tasks.  Write $s(i)\geq 0$
for the quantity of task~$i$ that AI contributes, with
\begin{equation}
  s'(i) < 0 \;\text{ for } i<I^*,
  \qquad
  s(i) = 0 \;\text{ for } i \geq I^*,
  \label{eq:ai_supply}
\end{equation}
where $I^*\in(0,1)$ is the \emph{AI capability frontier}.  Tasks below $I^*$
can be partially supplied by AI, with the contribution strictly decreasing in
task complexity; tasks at or above $I^*$ remain beyond AI's reach.  As AI
improves, $I^*$ shifts rightward and $s$ may increase throughout its support.

\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The output price $P_Y$ is exogenous and fixed.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Full Specialization]
  Workers are indexed by ability type $i^*\in[0,1]$.  A worker of type $i^*$ fully specializes in task
  $i^*$---the task at which they hold comparative
  advantage over other types.  Each task $i$ is therefore supplied exclusively by workers of
  type $i^*=i$.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Inelastic Labor Supply]
  The density of worker types $f(i)$ over $[0,1]$ is fixed and exogenous.  Workers do not
  adjust their type in response to wages, so the pre-AI supply of task $i$ is just $f(i)$
  regardless of the task price.
\end{assumption}

\noindent Competitive firms take $P_Y$ and task prices $\{p_0(i)\}$ as given and minimize the
cost of producing $Y$ subject to~\eqref{eq:ces}.  The equilibrium task prices and wages are derived
in Appendix~\ref{app:foc}.








\subsection{Pre-AI Wages}

Before AI, cost minimization subject to \eqref{eq:ces} yields equilibrium
task prices (derived in Appendix~\ref{app:foc}):
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = P_Y \cdot \left(\frac{Y_0}{f(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma},
  \label{eq:taskprice_preai}
\end{equation}
where $Y_0\equiv\bigl[\int_0^1 f(i)^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}di\bigr]^{\sigma/(\sigma-1)}$
is pre-AI aggregate output, fully determined by the distribution of worker
types $f$ via the CES aggregator. In this equilibrium, a
worker of type $i^*$ earns:
\begin{equation}
  w_0(i^*) = p_0(i^*).
  \label{eq:wage_preai}
\end{equation}
Figure~\ref{fig:preai} illustrates this wage schedule. The figure
plots $p_0(i)$ as linearly increasing in $i$ --- this is only for visual clarity, the model
imposes no explicit restriction on the shape of $p_0$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_preai.png}
  \caption{Pre-AI equilibrium wage schedule. Each worker of type $i^*$ earns the competitive task price $w_0(i^*) = p_0(i^*)$. The upward slope is assumed for illustration only; the model places no explicit restriction on the shape of $p_0(i)$.}
  \label{fig:preai}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Effects of the AI Supply Shock}

When AI enters the economy, it augments the supply side of each task
market it can serve.  The effective supply of task~$i$ becomes
\begin{equation}
  \bar y(i) \;=\; f(i) + s(i),
  \label{eq:effective_supply}
\end{equation}
where $s(i)>0$ for $i<I^*$ (AI contributes to supply) and $s(i)=0$
for $i\geq I^*$ (beyond AI's capability).  Because some task supplies
have risen while others have not, the CES aggregator yields a new
level of aggregate output
\begin{equation}
  \bar Y \;=\; \left[\int_0^1 \bar y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di
               \right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}}
  \;>\; Y_0,
  \label{eq:Ybar}
\end{equation}
and by the same cost-minimization logic as before
(Appendix~\ref{app:foc}), the post-AI task price is
\begin{equation}
  p_1(i) \;=\; P_Y\!\left(\frac{\bar Y}{\bar y(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
  \label{eq:taskprice_postai}
\end{equation}
One channel is set aside: \emph{augmentation} --- AI may eventually raise
the effective productivity of workers it complements rather than
supplements, altering the production technology itself.  This is left
for future work.

\paragraph{Post-AI wages.}
Since $w_1(i^*)=p_1(i^*)$, the post-AI wage is read directly from
\eqref{eq:taskprice_postai}:
\begin{equation}
  w_1(i^*) \;=\; P_Y\!\left(\frac{\bar Y}{f(i^*)+s(i^*)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
  \label{eq:wage_postai}
\end{equation}
The wage change relative to the pre-AI benchmark
$w_0(i^*)=P_Y\bigl(Y_0/f(i^*)\bigr)^{1/\sigma}$ is governed by
two competing movements: the numerator has grown ($\bar Y>Y_0$)
because AI adds productive capacity, while the denominator has grown
($f+s>f$) for tasks AI can supply.  The interaction of these two
forces partitions workers into three groups, examined in turn below.

\paragraph{Group 1: Workers above the frontier ($i^*\geq I^*$).}
For these workers $s(i^*)=0$, so the denominator is unchanged.
The wage ratio simplifies to
\begin{equation}
  \frac{w_1(i^*)}{w_0(i^*)}
  \;=\;
  \left(\frac{\bar Y}{Y_0}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}
  \;>\; 1.
  \label{eq:above_frontier}
\end{equation}
These workers \emph{unambiguously benefit}: the economy's expanded
output raises the marginal value of every task whose supply has not
changed.  The gain is uniform across all $i^*\geq I^*$ and
increasing in the aggregate size of the AI supply shock.

\paragraph{Group 2: Heavily exposed workers ($i^*<I^*$, large $s/f$).}
For workers deep inside $[0,I^*)$, AI supply $s(i^*)$ is large
relative to human supply $f(i^*)$.  The wage ratio becomes
\begin{equation}
  \frac{w_1(i^*)}{w_0(i^*)}
  \;=\;
  \left(\frac{\bar Y}{Y_0}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}
  \cdot
  \left(\frac{f(i^*)}{f(i^*)+s(i^*)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
  \label{eq:heavily_exposed}
\end{equation}
The first factor exceeds one (output expansion), but the second
factor is strictly less than one and shrinks as $s/f$ grows.
A worker experiences wage \emph{compression} whenever
\begin{equation}
  \frac{s(i^*)}{f(i^*)} \;>\; \frac{\bar Y}{Y_0} - 1,
  \label{eq:compression_threshold}
\end{equation}
i.e.\ whenever the proportional supply increase in their task exceeds
the proportional increase in aggregate output.  Because $\bar Y/Y_0$
reflects a CES average over \emph{all} tasks --- including the
majority where $s=0$ --- while $s(i^*)/f(i^*)$ is concentrated among
AI-supplied tasks, condition~\eqref{eq:compression_threshold} is
satisfied for workers whose tasks are heavily supplemented by AI.
These workers face unambiguous wage losses.

\paragraph{Group 3: Mildly exposed workers ($i^*<I^*$, small $s/f$).}
Near the frontier, $s(i^*)\to 0$ as $i^*\to I^*$ (by
continuity of $s$).  For these workers the second factor
in~\eqref{eq:heavily_exposed} is close to one, and the output
expansion factor dominates:
\[
  \frac{w_1(i^*)}{w_0(i^*)}
  \;\approx\;
  \left(\frac{\bar Y}{Y_0}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}
  \;>\; 1
  \qquad\text{when } s(i^*)/f(i^*) \text{ is small}.
\]
These workers \emph{gain} despite being below $I^*$.  There exists a
threshold task $\hat\imath < I^*$ at which the two effects exactly
offset:
\[
  \frac{s(\hat\imath)}{f(\hat\imath)}
  \;=\; \frac{\bar Y}{Y_0} - 1.
\]
Workers with $i^*>\hat\imath$ (including all of $[I^*,1]$)
experience wage gains; workers with $i^*<\hat\imath$ experience wage
compression.  The boundary between winners and losers therefore lies
\emph{strictly below} $I^*$, not at it.

\paragraph{Displacement.}
Workers anywhere in the distribution whose post-AI wage falls below a
reservation threshold exit employment; displacement is the
extensive-margin counterpart of wage compression.  As AI capability
improves ($I^*$ rises and $s$ increases), the set of compressed
workers expands and the threshold $\hat\imath$ shifts rightward
(Appendix~\ref{app:cutoff}).

Figure~\ref{fig:postai} illustrates these effects.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_postai.png}
  \caption{Post-AI shock under two AI supply intensities.  Left panel: task supply $f(i)$ (blue) and $f(i)+s(i)$ (dashed).  Right panel: pre-AI wage $w_0$ (dashed blue) vs.\ post-AI wage $w_1$ (solid).  Workers deep inside $[0,I^*)$ with large $s/f$ see wage compression; workers near $I^*$ with small $s/f$ can still gain from output expansion; workers above $I^*$ gain uniformly.}

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{CPS-ASEC}

The primary data source is the Annual Social and Economic Supplement to the Current Population Survey (CPS-ASEC), accessed through IPUMS \parencite{ipumscps2025}. The CPS-ASEC is a nationally representative annual survey that captures labor-force status, occupation, wage income, and detailed demographic characteristics for approximately 100,000 households. I use eight survey waves covering income reference years 2018--2025, spanning both the pre- and post-ChatGPT periods.

I restrict the sample to respondents aged 22--65 who are active in the labor force - either employed or actively seeking work. Workers with less than a high school diploma are excluded. After merging with the AI exposure variable and dropping observations with missing values, the main estimation sample contains 500,669 person-year observations.

\paragraph{Unemployment.} The unemployment indicator equals one if a respondent is classified as unemployed by the CPS - not working but actively searching for work (CPS \texttt{empstat} codes 21 and 22) - and zero otherwise. The sample mean is 3.4\%, roughly consistent with official U.S. unemployment statistics over this period.

\paragraph{Wages.} For the wage analysis, I use annual real wage and salary income (\texttt{incwage}), deflated to 1982 dollars using the annual average CPI-U from FRED \parencite{fred_cpiaucsl}. The outcome variable is the natural log of real wages. Observations with zero or missing wage income are excluded from the wage sample (workers who are unemployed or out of the labor force for wage purposes), yielding 464,300 observations.
\subsection{AI Exposure}

The AI exposure variable is drawn from \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}, which constructs occupation-level LLM exposure scores from the O*NET 27.2 database. O*NET provides two complementary task-level resources: 19,265 \emph{occupational tasks} (occupation-specific units of work tied to a particular occupation) and 2,087 \emph{Detailed Work Activities} (DWAs) --- broader action categories that may be associated with one or more tasks across occupations.

\paragraph{Exposure rubric.}
Annotators assess each task or DWA on a three-category rubric, asking whether access to an LLM could reduce the time required to complete it by at least 50\% at equivalent quality:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{E0} (no exposure): the LLM yields no meaningful time reduction, or degrades output quality.
  \item \textbf{E1} (direct exposure): a standard LLM interface---ChatGPT or the OpenAI Playground---reduces task time by at least 50\% on its own.
  \item \textbf{E2} (LLM-augmented exposure): the LLM alone cannot meet the threshold, but software built \emph{on top of} the LLM could do so.
\end{itemize}
Where DWA labels are available, they are first aggregated to the task level before proceeding to the occupation level.

\paragraph{Occupation-level aggregation.}
For each occupation, O*NET designates each task as \emph{core} (weight $w_t = 2$) or \emph{supplemental} (weight $w_t = 1$). Three summary measures vary how E1 and E2 tasks are scored:
\begin{equation}
  \text{Occupation Score} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{t \in T_o} w_t \cdot s_t}{\displaystyle\sum_{t \in T_o} w_t},
  \label{eq:beta}
\end{equation}
where $s_t = 1$ for E1 tasks, $s_t = 0.5$ for E2 tasks, and $s_t = 0$ for E0 tasks. The \emph{alpha} score ($\alpha_o$) sets $s_t = 0$ for all E2 tasks, counting only direct LLM exposure. The \emph{zeta} score ($\zeta_o$) sets $s_t = 1$ for E2 tasks, providing an upper bound. $\beta_o$ is the weighted middle ground: E2 tasks are discounted by half, reflecting the additional efforts required to build and deploy LLM-powered software on top of the base model.

\paragraph{Annotators.}
\textcite{eloundou2024gpts} collect two independent sets of annotations. \emph{Human ratings} --- produced by the authors and experienced alignment annotators --- are the primary benchmark; they were applied to DWAs and aggregated to the task level. \emph{GPT-4 ratings} were obtained by prompting an early GPT-4 version directly on all task/occupation pairs, serving as a validation dataset. The two sources are highly correlated at the occupation level.

I use \texttt{dv\_rating\_beta}: the \emph{GPT-4-annotated} $\beta$ score. Applying a single model uniformly across all task/occupation pairs ensures internal consistency. The cost is potential overconfidence in GPT-4's assessment of its own capabilities. $\beta$ is preferred to $\alpha$ (which ignores the ecosystem of LLM-powered tools already deployed or in development at the time of study) and $\zeta$ (which assumes a hypothetically well-developed AI infrastructure). $\beta$ is therefore the most economically meaningful measure for the post-ChatGPT period.

The variable ranges from approximately 0 (low exposure, e.g., Construction and Extraction) to 0.78 (high exposure, e.g., Computer and Mathematical, Legal, Business and Financial Operations), \textcolor{red}{with a sample standard deviation of roughly 0.17.Because $\beta_o \in [0,1]$, all regression coefficients are interpreted per 0.1-unit increase throughout. The variable is time-invariant and assigned at the occupational level.} 


Because CPS-ASEC codes occupations using a Census-based scheme which is distinct from the SOC occupation codes used by \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}, I construct a hierarchical crosswalk: from Census 2010 occupation codes to 6-digit 2010 SOC codes, then from 2010 SOC to 2018 SOC using the BLS official crosswalk \parencite{bls_soc_crosswalk_2018}. Where exact 6-digit matches are unavailable, exposure scores are aggregated at the 5-digit and 4-digit SOC levels, taking group means. This procedure retains nearly all observations in the sample.

\paragraph{Potential experience.} For the experience heterogeneity specification, I impute potential labor-market experience as $\max(\text{age} - \text{years of schooling} - 6,\, 0)$, with years of schooling assigned from the CPS education recode.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Main Specification}

The core empirical question is whether outcomes for workers in high-exposure occupations moved differently from outcomes in low-exposure occupations after ChatGPT's release, relative to how they moved before. The estimating equation is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $Y_{it}$ is either the unemployment indicator or log wages for worker $i$ in year $t$; $\text{AIExposure}_i$ is the worker i's occupation's AI exposure score; and year 2022 is the omitted category. The lower-order terms include year fixed effects and the main effect of AI exposure. The $\beta_t$ coefficients trace, year by year, whether high-exposure workers diverge from low-exposure workers relative to the 2022 baseline. If AI caused labor-market disruption after its diffusion, we expect the post-2022 $\beta_t$ to be positive (for unemployment) or negative (for wages), while the pre-2022 $\beta_t$ should hover near zero.

Four progressively stricter specifications are estimated: (1) no additional controls, (2) sex fixed effects, (3) sex and education fixed effects, and (4) sex, education, and state$\times$year two-way fixed effects. Specification (4) absorbs state-specific time shocks and is the preferred specification. Standard errors are clustered at the occupation level across all specifications.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: Experience and Education}

\paragraph{Experience.} To examine whether the effect varies by seniority of the worker, I supplement the main specification with a triple interaction:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:triple}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \delta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{Experience}_i \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it}.
\end{equation}
A negative post-2022 $\delta_t$ would indicate that more-experienced workers are relatively shielded from the influence of AI; a positive value would indicate they are disproportionately affected. 

\paragraph{Education.} For the education dimension I divide the sample into \emph{college-educated} workers (BA or above, educ $>$ 110) and \emph{non-college} workers (high school or some college, educ $\leq$ 110) and estimate the main specification \eqref{eq:main} separately within each group:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:split}
Y_{it}^{\,g} = \alpha^g
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t^g \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma^g + \varepsilon_{it}^g, \quad g \in \{\text{college},\;\text{non-college}\}.
\end{equation}
Each subsample has its own baseline, trend, and exposure gradient, and the two coefficient paths $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{col}}\}$ and $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{noncol}}\}$ can be compared directly.

\section{Results and Interpretation}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Unemployment}

Table~\ref{tab:unemployment_main} and Figure~\ref{fig:unemployment_stateYFE_plot} present the unemployment results. The pattern is clear and remarkably stable across all four specifications (see Figures~\ref{fig:unemployment_naive_plot}--\ref{fig:unemployment_sexeducfe_plot} for specifications (1)--(3)).

In the pre-ChatGPT period, coefficients for 2018 and 2019 are statistically indistinguishable from zero. The pandemic years (2020--2021) present a noticable negative: AI-exposed occupations experienced \emph{lower} unemployment during COVID-19, with coefficients of $-0.0213^{***}$ in 2020 and $-0.0353^{***}$ in 2021. This reflects the simple fact that high-exposure occupations - legal, financial, managerial, computational - were largely remote / hybrid -compatible and thus are partly shielded from the layoffs that consentrated in the in-person service occupations.

The post-2022 reversal is the central finding. The 2023 coefficient is small and insignificant ($+0.002$), consistent with a natural lag between GPT model release and meaningful workforce adjustment. By 2024, the coefficient rises to $+0.0205^{***}$ and remains at $+0.0180^{***}$ in 2025. A 0.1 increase in exposure is associated with a 0.2 percentage point increase in unemployment probability, corresponding to roughly a 6\% increase relative to the 3.4\% baseline. This effect is robust across all four specifications, including the most demanding one that absorbs state-specific aggregate shocks.

%==================================================
\subsection{Wages}
%==================================================

Table~\ref{tab:logwage_main} presents the log-wage results. Pre-ChatGPT coefficients are uniformly small and statistically insignificant across all four specifications, confirming parallel wage trends. In the post-2022 period, the naive and sex-FE specifications yield 2025 coefficients of $-0.036$ and $-0.046$, neither significant at conventional levels. Only the most demanding specification --- absorbing sex, education, and state$\times$year fixed effects --- produces a statistically significant 2025 compression of $-0.059^{**}$. No significant effect appears in 2023 or 2024 under any specification. This weak and late-emerging signal suggests that wage adjustment, if any, lags behind the employment adjustment above and requires absorbing state-level aggregate shocks to emerge clearly.




%==================================================
\subsection{Who Bears the Burden: Education Heterogeneity}
%==================================================

\paragraph{Unemployment.}

Splitting the sample by education reveals a symmetric picture for unemployment. Both college-educated workers (BA or above; baseline unemployment rate 2.2\%) and non-college workers (high school or some college; baseline 4.3\%) in AI-exposed occupations experienced comparable increases in unemployment risk after 2022. For college workers, the post-2022 coefficients are $+0.018^{**}$ in 2024 and $+0.018^{**}$ in 2025, stable across all four specifications. For non-college workers, the corresponding estimates are $+0.020^{***}$ in 2024 and $+0.014^{*}$ in 2025. The point estimates are similar in magnitude and the confidence intervals overlap. Hence there is no statistically significant difference in the unemployment response across education groups. Regarding pre-2022 trends: the 2018 and 2019 coefficients are near zero and insignificant for both groups. The 2020--2021 coefficients are significantly negative in both subsamples - mirroring the main-spec finding that high-exposure occupations shed less unemployment during COVID-19 - and should be read as a replication of that pattern within each education group rather than as evidence against identification. Specifications (1)--(3) yield nearly identical results (Figures~\ref{fig:unemp_colComb_naive}--\ref{fig:unemp_colComb_sexeducFE}).

The wage results reveal a divergence across education groups. \subsection{Experience Heterogeneity}
%==================================================

\paragraph{Unemployment.}

Table~\ref{tab:unemp_exphet} reports the unemployment experience-heterogeneity results. The triple-interaction coefficients on year $\times$ experience $\times$ AI exposure are economically small and statistically insignificant across all post-2022 years and all three specifications in both subsamples. For college workers, 2024 and 2025 coefficients are approximately $+0.0007$ with standard errors of similar magnitude. For non-college workers, post-2022 estimates are even smaller (around $\pm 0.0002$) and similarly insignificant. Within either education group, there is no evidence that more-experienced workers in AI-exposed occupations faced systematically different unemployment risk.


\paragraph{Log Wages.}

The log-wage experience results are largely null: post-2022 triple-interaction coefficients are uniformly small and statistically insignificant across all four specifications for college workers. The borderline-significant 2025 estimate in column (3) ($-0.0076^{*}$) disappears once state$\times$year fixed effects are added (column (4): $-0.0072$, n.s.). For non-college workers, the 2023 coefficient of $-0.0043^{**}$ persists across all four specifications, suggesting that more-experienced non-college workers in AI-exposed occupations faced greater wage compression immediately after ChatGPT's release, but the effect does not persist into 2024 or 2025. Interpretation is complicated by significant pre-period coefficients in 2019 ($-0.0042^{**}$) and 2021 ($-0.0065^{***}$) for the non-college subsample, indicating that the experience--AI-wage relationship was not flat before 2022 and the 2023 signal cannot be attributed cleanly to the post-ChatGPT treatment.

\section{Derivation of Wage Schedules}
\label{app:foc}

\noindent\textbf{Step 1.} Cost minimization $\to$ FOC:
$\;p_0(i)=\mu\cdot(Y/y(i))^{1/\sigma}$, \quad $\mu=P_Y$ in competitive equilibrium.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 2.} Task demand:
$\;y^d(i)=Y(P_Y/p_0(i))^{\sigma}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 3.} Market clearing $y^d(i)=f(i)$:
\[
  p_0(i) = P_Y\!\left(\frac{Y}{f(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
\]

\noindent\textbf{Step 4.} Plug $y(i)=f(i)$ into CES $\to$ output is pinned:
\[
  Y_0 = \left[\int_0^1 f(i)^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}\,di
  \right]^{\!\sigma/(\sigma-1)}.
\]
$Y_0$ depends only on worker distribution $f$ --- not on prices. $\blacksquare$

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Step 5.} Post-AI: effective supply
$\bar y(i)=f(i)+s(i)$.  Repeat Steps 1--4 with $\bar y$ in place of $f$:
\[
  \bar Y = \left[\int_0^1 (f(i)+s(i))^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}\,di
  \right]^{\!\sigma/(\sigma-1)} > Y_0,
  \qquad
  p_1(i) = P_Y\!\left(\frac{\bar Y}{f(i)+s(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma}.
  \quad\blacksquare
\]

\section{The AI Capability Frontier}
\label{app:cutoff}

The AI supply function $s(i)$ is positive on $[0,I^*)$ and zero
on $[I^*,1]$, with $s'(i)<0$ for $i<I^*$ (see~\eqref{eq:ai_supply}).
The parameter $I^*$ indexes the frontier of AI capability: higher
$I^*$ means AI can supply a wider range of tasks.


As $I^*$ increases (holding the shape of $s$ fixed), two reinforcing
effects operate.  First, a wider set $[0,I^*)$ of workers is directly
exposed to supply dilution.  Second, $\bar Y$ rises further, but
the additional output gain is spread across all tasks while the
supply-dilution penalty concentrates on the newly automated range.
The net effect is that wage compression deepens for workers well
inside $[0,I^*)$ and extends to tasks that were previously beyond
AI's reach.  $\blacksquare$

Figure~\ref{fig:capability} illustrates the expanding frontier.

  \centering

  \caption{Increasing AI capability: the automation frontier expands. As $I^*$ rises (AI improves), a wider range of tasks receives AI supply, strengthening the supply-dilution channel for exposed workers while raising $\bar Y$ for all.}
