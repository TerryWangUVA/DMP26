\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Technology, Tasks, and Labor Markets}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{acemoglu2019automation})}
    Develops a theoretical model explaining how automation both displaces existing tasks and creates new ones.
    Highlights the ambiguous effects of technology on labor demand depending on whether new tasks are complements or substitutes for human labor.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Measuring Exposure to Generative AI}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2018method})}
    Introduces a quantitative approach linking progress in distinct AI subfields (from the EFF AI Progress metrics) to human abilities defined in O*NET.
    Maps AI capabilities $\rightarrow$ O*NET abilities $\rightarrow$ occupations.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2021aioe})}
    Extends the 2018 framework to a forward-looking exposure index using Mechanical Turk surveys to map ten AI applications to 52 O*NET abilities.
    Aggregates ability-level exposure (weighted by importance and prevalence) to occupation, industry, and geographic levels.
    Produces standardized exposure scores such as \textit{AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE)}, \textit{AI Industry Exposure (AIIE)}, and \textit{AI Geography Exposure (AIGE)}, measuring potential technical exposure rather than realized adoption.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{webb2020impact})}
    Constructs a task-technology overlap model matching verbs and nouns from patent text to O*NET task descriptions.
    Identifies high-skill cognitive tasks (judgment, analysis) as most AI-exposed, contrasting with robotics' focus on routine manual work.
    Provides a scalable, data-driven mapping of AI technologies to tasks using textual similarity.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{eloundou2024gpts})}
    Uses GPT-4 and human annotators to classify O*NET tasks into categories based on whether LLMs could reduce task completion time by at least 50\%. Aggregates task labels to occupation-level exposure scores and produces composite indices.
    Provides a large-scale, LLM-specific exposure dataset reflecting technical feasibility rather than observed adoption.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the Evidence Shows So Far}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{hosseini2025seniority})}
Examines whether generative AI constitutes a seniority-biased technological change, disproportionately affecting junior relative to senior workers.
Combines LinkedIn résumé data (Revelio Labs) with nearly 200 million job postings. Measures firm-level AI adoption by identifying postings for ``GenAI integrator'' roles responsible for implementing or integrating AI tools. Employs a difference-in-differences framework comparing junior versus senior employment in adopting versus non-adopting firms before and after 2023.
Finds that after ChatGPT's release, junior employment in adopting firms declined by roughly 9--10\%, while senior employment remained stable, with effects concentrated in high-exposure occupations.  Provides early empirical evidence that generative AI adoption shifts firm workforce composition toward senior labor.
    \item \textbf{(\cite{brynjolfsson2025canaries})}
Uses high-frequency administrative payroll data from ADP, covering millions of U.S. workers, to document six stylized facts about the early labor market effects of generative AI.
Identifies that employment among early-career workers (ages 22--25) in highly AI-exposed occupations has declined by roughly 13\% since late 2022, even after controlling for firm-level shocks.
Employment for older or less AI-exposed workers remained stable or continued to grow.
Shows that employment declines are concentrated in occupations where AI automates rather than augments work, based on task-level measures from the Anthropic Economic Index and GPT-4 exposure (Eloundou et al., 2024).
Finds limited wage effects, suggesting adjustments occurred primarily through employment rather than compensation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Theoretical Framework}
\label{sec:model}
%==================================================

\subsection{Setup}

Production requires a continuum of cognitive tasks indexed $i \in [0,1]$,
ordered by susceptibility to AI automation. Tasks at the low end are routine cognitive tasks easily replicated by large language models; tasks at the high end require human expertise that AI currently struggles to replicate.


Final output $Y$ aggregates tasks via a CES technology:
\begin{equation}
  Y = \left[\int_0^1 y(i)^{\frac{\sigma-1}{\sigma}}\,di\right]^{\!\frac{\sigma}{\sigma-1}},
  \qquad \sigma > 0,
  \label{eq:ces}
\end{equation}
where $y(i)$ is the quantity of task $i$ used in production.  Each task can
additionally be performed by AI at unit cost
\begin{equation}
  c^{\mathrm{AI}}(i) = \tau\cdot t(i), \qquad t(i) = e^{\alpha i},\quad \alpha>0,
  \label{eq:ai_cost}
\end{equation}
where $\tau > 0$ is an aggregate AI capability parameter (lower $\tau$ reflects more capable AI), and $t(i)$ is strictly increasing and exponential in task complexity (i.e., $t'(i) > 0$ and $t''(i) > 0$), ensuring that more complex tasks are increasingly costly to automate.
\begin{assumption}[Small Open Economy]
  The output price $P_Y$ is exogenous and fixed.
\end{assumption}

\begin{assumption}[Full Specialization]
  Workers are indexed by ability type $i^*\in[0,1]$.  A worker of type $i^*$
  is capable of performing any task $i\leq i^*$ but fully specializes in task
  $i^*$---the hardest task they can perform, at which they hold comparative
  advantage.  Each task $i$ is therefore supplied exclusively by workers of
  type $i^*=i$.
\end{assumption}



\subsection{Pre-AI Wages}

Before AI, cost minimization subject to \eqref{eq:ces} yields equilibrium
task prices (derived in Appendix~\ref{app:foc}):
\begin{equation}
  p_0(i) = P_Y \cdot \left(\frac{Y}{y_0(i)}\right)^{\!1/\sigma},
  \label{eq:taskprice_preai}
\end{equation}
where $y_0(i)=f(i)$ is the pre-AI supply of task $i$, equal to the density
of worker types under full specialization. In this equilibrium, a
worker of type $i^*$ earns:
\begin{equation}
  w_0(i^*) = p_0(i^*).
  \label{eq:wage_preai}
\end{equation}

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_preai.png}
  \caption{Pre-AI equilibrium wage schedule. Each worker of type $i^*$ earns the competitive task price $w_0(i^*) = p_0(i^*)$. }
  \label{fig:preai}
\end{figure}

\subsection{AI Shock, Cutoff, and Wage Compression}

Post-AI, competition between human and AI providers drives task prices to:
\begin{equation}
  p(i) = \min\!\bigl(p_0(i),\;\tau e^{\alpha i}\bigr).
  \label{eq:taskprice_postai}
\end{equation}
Under the single-crossing condition maintained in Appendix~\ref{app:cutoff},
there exists a unique threshold $I^*$ satisfying:
\begin{equation}
  \tau\,e^{\alpha I^*} = p_0(I^*).
  \label{eq:cutoff}
\end{equation}
Tasks $i<I^*$ are automated; tasks $i\geq I^*$ remain performed by humans.
As AI improves ($\tau\downarrow$), $I^*$ rises.  The wage change for worker
$i^*$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \Delta w(i^*) =
  \begin{cases}
    \tau e^{\alpha i^*} - p_0(i^*) < 0 & \text{if }i^* < I^*, \\[4pt]
    0 & \text{if }i^* \geq I^*.
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:deltaw}
\end{equation}
The sign for $i^*<I^*$ is unambiguous: by definition of the cutoff,
$\tau e^{\alpha i^*}<p_0(i^*)$ throughout the automated range.  Workers
below the cutoff suffer wage compression proportional to how far AI cost
undershoots their pre-AI task price; workers above it are unaffected in
partial equilibrium.  Workers whose compressed wage falls below a reservation
threshold exit employment---displacement is the extensive-margin counterpart
of wage compression.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_postai.png}
  \caption{Post-AI shock: $I^*$ and wage compression. The AI cost curve $\tau e^{\alpha i}$ (dotted red) undercuts the pre-AI task price $p_0(i)$ (dashed blue) for tasks below the cutoff $I^*$. The solid green line is the effective post-AI price $p(i)=\min(p_0(i),\tau e^{\alpha i})$. Workers below $I^*$ experience wage compression $\Delta w < 0$; workers above are unaffected.}
  \label{fig:postai}
\end{figure}

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{CPS-ASEC}

The primary data source is the Annual Social and Economic Supplement to the Current Population Survey (CPS-ASEC), accessed through IPUMS. The CPS-ASEC is a nationally representative annual survey that captures labor-force status, occupation, wage income, and detailed demographic characteristics for approximately 100,000 households. I use eight survey waves covering income reference years 2018--2025, spanning both the pre- and post-ChatGPT periods.

I restrict the sample to respondents aged 22--65 who are active in the labor force - either employed or actively seeking work. Workers with less than a high school diploma are excluded. After merging with the AI exposure variable and dropping observations with missing values, the main estimation sample contains 500,669 person-year observations.

\paragraph{Unemployment.} The unemployment indicator equals one if a respondent is classified as unemployed by the CPS - not working but actively searching for work (CPS \texttt{empstat} codes 21 and 22) - and zero otherwise. The sample mean is 3.4\%, roughly consistent with official U.S. unemployment statistics over this period.

\paragraph{Wages.} For the wage analysis, I use annual real wage and salary income (\texttt{incwage}), deflated to 1982 dollars using the annual average CPI-U from FRED. The outcome variable is the natural log of real wages. Observations with zero or missing wage income are excluded from the wage sample (workers who are unemployed or out of the labor force for wage purposes), yielding 464,300 observations.

\subsection{AI Exposure}

The AI exposure variable is the GPT-4 annotator beta score (\texttt{dv\_rating\_beta}) from \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}. \textcite{eloundou2024gpts} measure occupation-level exposure to large language models using a task-based annotation methodology. Drawing on the O*NET task inventory, trained human annotators and GPT-4 itself independently assessed each task in each occupation according to whether access to a GPT-4-type system could reduce the time required to complete that task by at least 50\%. Scores are then aggregated to the occupational level as the share of tasks judged highly susceptible to LLM substitution or augmentation.

The study produces three variants of the score corresponding to three assumptions about the technological capability frontier. The \emph{alpha} score (most conservative) assumes access to the bare GPT-4 API only, with no additional software tools. The \emph{gamma} score (most expansive) imagines hypothetical future software that leverages GPT-4 to its full potential. The \emph{beta} score occupies the middle ground: it assumes access to GPT-4 together with plausible near-term software complements - such as code interpreters and retrieval-augmented search - that were realistic at the time of writing. I use beta as the main exposure measure. It is a balance between the overly conservative alpha (which ignores the ecosystem of applications already built around LLMs) and the speculative gamma (which conflates current AI with a hypothetically well-developed infrastructure). Beta is therefore the most economically meaningful measure for studying the labor-market impact of generative AI as it has actually been deployed.

The variable runs from approximately 0 (low exposure, e.g., Construction and Extraction Occupations) to approximately 0.78 (high exposure, e.g., Computer and Mathematical, Legal, Business and Financial Operations Occupations), with a sample standard deviation of roughly 0.17. Because the scale is bounded within $[0,1]$, regression coefficients are best interpreted per 0.1-unit increase in the beta score rather than per unit. All coefficient interpretations in this paper use 0.1-unit increments unless otherwise stated. The variable is time-invariant and assigned at the occupational level.

Because the CPS-ASEC codes occupations using a Census-based scheme distinct from the SOC codes used by \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}, I construct a hierarchical crosswalk: from Census 2010 occupation codes to 6-digit 2010 SOC codes, then from 2010 SOC to 2018 SOC using the BLS official crosswalk. Where exact 6-digit matches are unavailable, exposure scores are aggregated at the 5-digit and 4-digit SOC levels, taking group means. This procedure retains nearly all observations in the sample.

\paragraph{Potential experience.} For the experience heterogeneity specification, I impute potential labor-market experience as $\max(\text{age} - \text{years of schooling} - 6,\, 0)$, with years of schooling assigned from the CPS education recode.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Main Specification}

The core empirical question is whether outcomes for workers in high-exposure occupations moved differently from outcomes in low-exposure occupations after ChatGPT's release, relative to how they moved before. The estimating equation is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $Y_{it}$ is either the unemployment indicator or log wages for worker $i$ in year $t$; $\text{AIExposure}_i$ is the time-invariant exposure score; and year 2022 is the omitted category. The $\beta_t$ coefficients trace, year by year, whether high-exposure workers diverge from low-exposure workers relative to the 2022 baseline. If AI caused labor-market disruption after its diffusion, we expect the post-2022 $\beta_t$ to be positive (for unemployment) or negative (for wages), while the pre-2022 $\beta_t$ should hover near zero.

Four progressively richer specifications are estimated: (1) no additional controls, (2) sex fixed effects, (3) sex and education fixed effects, and (4) sex, education, and state$\times$year fixed effects. Specification (4) absorbs state-specific time shocks and is the preferred specification. Standard errors are clustered at the occupation level throughout.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: Experience and Education}

\paragraph{Experience.} To examine whether the effect varies by seniority, I supplement the main specification with a triple interaction:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:triple}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \delta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{Experience}_i \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it}.
\end{equation}
A negative post-2022 $\delta_t$ would indicate that more-experienced workers are relatively shielded from the influence of AI; a positive value would indicate they are disproportionately affected. 

\paragraph{Education.} For the education dimension I divide the sample into \emph{college-educated} workers (BA or above, educ $>$ 110) and \emph{non-college} workers (high school or some college, educ $\leq$ 110) and estimate the main specification \eqref{eq:main} separately within each group:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:split}
Y_{it}^{\,g} = \alpha^g
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t^g \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma^g + \varepsilon_{it}^g, \quad g \in \{\text{college},\;\text{non-college}\}.
\end{equation}
This approach is more transparent than a pooled regression with triple interactions. Each subsample has its own baseline, trend, and exposure gradient, and the two coefficient paths $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{col}}\}$ and $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{noncol}}\}$ can be compared directly on a combined coefficient plot.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Unemployment}

Table~\ref{tab:unemployment_main} and Figure~\ref{fig:unemployment_stateYFE_plot} present the unemployment results. The pattern is clear and remarkably stable across all four specifications (see Figures~\ref{fig:unemployment_naive_plot}--\ref{fig:unemployment_sexeducfe_plot} for specifications (1)--(3)).

In the pre-ChatGPT period, coefficients for 2018 and 2019 are statistically indistinguishable from zero. The pandemic years (2020--2021) present a noticable negative: AI-exposed occupations experienced \emph{lower} unemployment during COVID-19, with coefficients of $-0.0213^{***}$ in 2020 and $-0.0353^{***}$ in 2021. This reflects the simple fact that high-exposure occupations - legal, financial, managerial, computational - were largely remote / hybrid -compatible and thus are partly shielded from the layoffs that consentrated in the in-person service occupations.

The post-2022 reversal is the central finding. The 2023 coefficient is small and insignificant ($+0.002$), consistent with a natural lag between GPT model release and meaningful workforce adjustment. By 2024, the coefficient rises to $+0.0205^{***}$ and remains at $+0.0180^{***}$ in 2025. A 0.1 increase in exposure is associated with a 0.2 percentage point increase in unemployment probability, corresponding to roughly a 6\% increase relative to the 3.4\% baseline. This effect is robust across all four specifications, including the most demanding one that absorbs state-specific aggregate shocks.

\subsection{Wages}
%==================================================

Table~\ref{tab:logwage_main} presents the log-wage results. Pre-ChatGPT coefficients are uniformly small and statistically insignificant across all four specifications, confirming parallel wage trends. In the post-2022 period, the naive and sex-FE specifications yield 2025 coefficients of $-0.036$ and $-0.046$, neither significant at conventional levels. No significant effect appears in 2023 or 2024 under any specification. This weak and late-emerging signal suggests that wage adjustment, if any, lags behind the employment adjustment above and requires absorbing state-level aggregate shocks to emerge clearly.

\subsection{Who Bears the Burden: Education Heterogeneity}

\paragraph{Unemployment.}

Splitting the sample by education reveals a symmetric picture for unemployment. Both college-educated workers (BA or above; baseline unemployment rate 2.2\%) and non-college workers (high school or some college; baseline 4.3\%) in AI-exposed occupations experienced comparable increases in unemployment risk after 2022. For college workers, the post-2022 coefficients are $+0.018^{**}$ in 2024 and $+0.018^{**}$ in 2025, stable across all four specifications. \paragraph{Unemployment.}

Tables~\ref{tab:unemp_exphet_col} and \ref{tab:unemp_exphet_noncol} report the unemployment experience-heterogeneity results. \section{Derivation of the Pre-AI Task Price}
\label{app:foc}

Competitive firms minimize $\int_0^1 p_0(i)\,y(i)\,di$ subject to
\eqref{eq:ces}, taking $P_Y$ and task prices $\{p_0(i)\}$ as given.  Writing
$Y=Z^{\sigma/(\sigma-1)}$ with $Z\equiv\int_0^1 y(j)^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}dj$,
the first-order condition for task $i$ is $p_0(i)=\mu\cdot(Y/y(i))^{1/\sigma}$,
where the chain rule gives $\partial Y/\partial y(i)=Z^{1/(\sigma-1)}y(i)^{-1/\sigma}
=Y^{1/\sigma}y(i)^{-1/\sigma}$ (using $Z=Y^{(\sigma-1)/\sigma}$).
In competitive equilibrium $\mu=P_Y$, so the task demand function is
$y_0^d(i)=Y\cdot(P_Y/p_0(i))^\sigma$.
Under full specialization (Assumption~2), supply of task $i$ is $y_0(i)=f(i)$.
Market clearing $y_0^d(i)=y_0(i)$ then yields \eqref{eq:taskprice_preai}
directly. $\blacksquare$

\section{The Automation Cutoff: Existence, Uniqueness, and Comparative Static}
\label{app:cutoff}

Define $\phi(i)\equiv\tau e^{\alpha i}-p_0(i)$.  The automated set is
$\mathcal{A}\equiv\{i\in[0,1]:\phi(i)<0\}$.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Existence.}  Assuming $\phi(0)<0$ and $\phi(1)>0$,
continuity of $\phi$ guarantees by the intermediate value theorem that at
least one crossing $I^*\in(0,1)$ with $\phi(I^*)=0$ exists.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Single-crossing assumption.}  Without restrictions
on $p_0$, $\phi$ may cross zero multiple times, making $\mathcal{A}$ a union
of intervals rather than $[0,I^*)$.  We maintain a single-crossing condition
as a tractable benchmark: $\phi$ crosses zero exactly once, so
$\mathcal{A}=[0,I^*)$ for a unique $I^*$.  In the general case, the wage
compression result \eqref{eq:deltaw} continues to hold for any
$i^*\in\mathcal{A}$, but affected workers need not be concentrated at the
low end of the task distribution.

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Comparative static.}  Totally differentiating
$\tau e^{\alpha I^*}=p_0(I^*)$ with respect to $\tau$:
\[
  \frac{\partial I^*}{\partial\tau}
  = \frac{e^{\alpha I^*}}{p_0'(I^*)-\alpha\,p_0(I^*)}.
\]
This is negative whenever $p_0'(I^*)<\alpha\,p_0(I^*)$, i.e.\
$\phi'(I^*)=\alpha\tau e^{\alpha I^*}-p_0'(I^*)>0$.  Under single-crossing
with $\phi(0)<0$ and $\phi(1)>0$, the unique crossing must have
$\phi'(I^*)>0$, so the condition is automatically satisfied.  Hence a fall
in $\tau$ raises $I^*$. $\blacksquare$

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_capability.png}
  \caption{Increasing AI capability: the automation frontier expands. As $\tau$ falls (AI improves), the cutoff $I^*$ shifts rightward, enlarging the automated set $\mathcal{A}=[0,I^*)$.}
  \label{fig:capability}
\end{figure}

\medskip\noindent\textbf{Multiple crossings.}  The comparative static
extends to the multiple-crossing case without modification.  At each zero
$I_k^*$ of $\phi$, the same formula applies: left boundaries of
$\mathcal{A}$ (upward crossings, $\phi'(I_k^*)>0$) satisfy
$\partial I_k^*/\partial\tau<0$ and shift rightward as $\tau$ falls, while
right boundaries (downward crossings, $\phi'(I_k^*)<0$) satisfy
$\partial I_k^*/\partial\tau>0$ and shift leftward.  In both cases the
automated set $\mathcal{A}$ expands, so the qualitative result---AI
improvements expand automation---holds regardless of the number of
crossings.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.82\textwidth]{output/model/fig_multiple.png}
  \caption{Multiple crossings: the automated set $\mathcal{A}$ as a union of intervals. When $p_0(i)$ is non-monotone, $\phi(i)=\tau e^{\alpha i}-p_0(i)$ may cross zero more than once, producing $\mathcal{A}=[0,I_1^*]\cup[I_2^*,I_3^*]$. Wage compression applies to all workers in $\mathcal{A}$, not only those at the low end of the task distribution.}
  \label{fig:multiple}
\end{figure}
