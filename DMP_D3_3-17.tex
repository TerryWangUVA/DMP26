\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{Technology, Tasks, and Labor Markets}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{acemoglu2019automation})}
    Develops a theoretical model explaining how automation both displaces existing tasks and creates new ones.
    Highlights the ambiguous effects of technology on labor demand depending on whether new tasks are complements or substitutes for human labor.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Measuring Exposure to Generative AI}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2018method})}
    Introduces a quantitative approach linking progress in distinct AI subfields (from the EFF AI Progress metrics) to human abilities defined in O*NET.
    Maps AI capabilities $\rightarrow$ O*NET abilities $\rightarrow$ occupations.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{felten2021aioe})}
    Extends the 2018 framework to a forward-looking exposure index using Mechanical Turk surveys to map ten AI applications to 52 O*NET abilities.
    Aggregates ability-level exposure (weighted by importance and prevalence) to occupation, industry, and geographic levels.
    Produces standardized exposure scores such as \textit{AI Occupational Exposure (AIOE)}, \textit{AI Industry Exposure (AIIE)}, and \textit{AI Geography Exposure (AIGE)}, measuring potential technical exposure rather than realized adoption.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{webb2020impact})}
    Constructs a task-technology overlap model matching verbs and nouns from patent text to O*NET task descriptions.
    Identifies high-skill cognitive tasks (judgment, analysis) as most AI-exposed, contrasting with robotics' focus on routine manual work.
    Provides a scalable, data-driven mapping of AI technologies to tasks using textual similarity.

    \item \textbf{(\cite{eloundou2024gpts})}
    Uses GPT-4 and human annotators to classify O*NET tasks into categories based on whether LLMs could reduce task completion time by at least 50\%. Aggregates task labels to occupation-level exposure scores and produces composite indices.
    Provides a large-scale, LLM-specific exposure dataset reflecting technical feasibility rather than observed adoption.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{What the Evidence Shows So Far}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
    \item \textbf{(\cite{hosseini2025seniority})}
Examines whether generative AI constitutes a seniority-biased technological change, disproportionately affecting junior relative to senior workers.
Combines LinkedIn résumé data (Revelio Labs) with nearly 200 million job postings. Measures firm-level AI adoption by identifying postings for ``GenAI integrator'' roles responsible for implementing or integrating AI tools. Employs a difference-in-differences framework comparing junior versus senior employment in adopting versus non-adopting firms before and after 2023.
Finds that after ChatGPT's release, junior employment in adopting firms declined by roughly 9--10\%, while senior employment remained stable, with effects concentrated in high-exposure occupations.  Provides early empirical evidence that generative AI adoption shifts firm workforce composition toward senior labor.
    \item \textbf{(\cite{brynjolfsson2025canaries})}
Uses high-frequency administrative payroll data from ADP, covering millions of U.S. workers, to document six stylized facts about the early labor market effects of generative AI.
Identifies that employment among early-career workers (ages 22--25) in highly AI-exposed occupations has declined by roughly 13\% since late 2022, even after controlling for firm-level shocks.
Employment for older or less AI-exposed workers remained stable or continued to grow.
Shows that employment declines are concentrated in occupations where AI automates rather than augments work, based on task-level measures from the Anthropic Economic Index and GPT-4 exposure (Eloundou et al., 2024).
Finds limited wage effects, suggesting adjustments occurred primarily through employment rather than compensation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}

\subsection{CPS-ASEC}

The primary data source is the Annual Social and Economic Supplement to the Current Population Survey (CPS-ASEC), accessed through IPUMS. The CPS-ASEC is a nationally representative annual survey that captures labor-force status, occupation, wage income, and detailed demographic characteristics for approximately 100,000 households. I use eight survey waves covering income reference years 2018--2025, spanning both the pre- and post-ChatGPT periods.

I restrict the sample to respondents aged 22--65 who are active in the labor force - either employed or actively seeking work. Workers with less than a high school diploma are excluded. After merging with the AI exposure variable and dropping observations with missing values, the main estimation sample contains 500,669 person-year observations.

\paragraph{Unemployment.} The unemployment indicator equals one if a respondent is classified as unemployed by the CPS - not working but actively searching for work (CPS \texttt{empstat} codes 21 and 22) - and zero otherwise. The sample mean is 3.4\%, roughly consistent with official U.S. unemployment statistics over this period.

\paragraph{Wages.} For the wage analysis, I use annual real wage and salary income (\texttt{incwage}), deflated to 1982 dollars using the annual average CPI-U from FRED. The outcome variable is the natural log of real wages. Observations with zero or missing wage income are excluded from the wage sample (workers who are unemployed or out of the labor force for wage purposes), yielding 464,300 observations.

\subsection{AI Exposure}

The AI exposure variable is the GPT-4 annotator beta score (\texttt{dv\_rating\_beta}) from \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}. \textcite{eloundou2024gpts} measure occupation-level exposure to large language models using a task-based annotation methodology. Drawing on the O*NET task inventory, trained human annotators and GPT-4 itself independently assessed each task in each occupation according to whether access to a GPT-4-type system could reduce the time required to complete that task by at least 50\%. Scores are then aggregated to the occupational level as the share of tasks judged highly susceptible to LLM substitution or augmentation.

The study produces three variants of the score corresponding to three assumptions about the technological capability frontier. The \emph{alpha} score (most conservative) assumes access to the bare GPT-4 API only, with no additional software tools. The \emph{gamma} score (most expansive) imagines hypothetical future software that leverages GPT-4 to its full potential. The \emph{beta} score occupies the middle ground: it assumes access to GPT-4 together with plausible near-term software complements - such as code interpreters and retrieval-augmented search - that were realistic at the time of writing. I use beta as the main exposure measure. It is a balance between the overly conservative alpha (which ignores the ecosystem of applications already built around LLMs) and the speculative gamma (which conflates current AI with a hypothetically well-developed infrastructure). Beta is therefore the most economically meaningful measure for studying the labor-market impact of generative AI as it has actually been deployed.

The variable runs from approximately 0 (low exposure, e.g., Construction and Extraction Occupations) to approximately 0.78 (high exposure, e.g., Computer and Mathematical, Legal, Business and Financial Operations Occupations), with a sample standard deviation of roughly 0.17. Because the scale is bounded within $[0,1]$, regression coefficients are best interpreted per 0.1-unit increase in the beta score rather than per unit. All coefficient interpretations in this paper use 0.1-unit increments unless otherwise stated. The variable is time-invariant and assigned at the occupational level.

Because the CPS-ASEC codes occupations using a Census-based scheme distinct from the SOC codes used by \textcite{eloundou2024gpts}, I construct a hierarchical crosswalk: from Census 2010 occupation codes to 6-digit 2010 SOC codes, then from 2010 SOC to 2018 SOC using the BLS official crosswalk. Where exact 6-digit matches are unavailable, exposure scores are aggregated at the 5-digit and 4-digit SOC levels, taking group means. This procedure retains nearly all observations in the sample.

\paragraph{Potential experience.} For the experience heterogeneity specification, I impute potential labor-market experience as $\max(\text{age} - \text{years of schooling} - 6,\, 0)$, with years of schooling assigned from the CPS education recode.

\section{Empirical Strategy}
\label{sec:strategy}

\subsection{Main Specification}

The core empirical question is whether outcomes for workers in high-exposure occupations moved differently from outcomes in low-exposure occupations after ChatGPT's release, relative to how they moved before. The estimating equation is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:main}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it},
\end{equation}
where $Y_{it}$ is either the unemployment indicator or log wages for worker $i$ in year $t$; $\text{AIExposure}_i$ is the time-invariant exposure score; and year 2022 is the omitted category. The $\beta_t$ coefficients trace, year by year, whether high-exposure workers diverge from low-exposure workers relative to the 2022 baseline. If AI caused labor-market disruption after its diffusion, we expect the post-2022 $\beta_t$ to be positive (for unemployment) or negative (for wages), while the pre-2022 $\beta_t$ should hover near zero.

Three progressively richer specifications are estimated: (1) no additional controls, (2) sex fixed effects, and (3) sex and education fixed effects. Standard errors are clustered at the occupation level throughout.

\subsection{Heterogeneity: Experience and Education}

\paragraph{Experience.} To examine whether the effect varies by seniority, I augment the main specification with a triple interaction:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:triple}
Y_{it} = \alpha
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \delta_t \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{Experience}_i \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + \text{lower-order terms} + X_{it}'\Gamma + \varepsilon_{it}.
\end{equation}
A negative post-2022 $\delta_t$ would indicate that more-experienced workers are relatively shielded from the influence of AI; a positive value would indicate they are disproportionately affected. 

\paragraph{Education: split-sample approach.} For the education dimension I divide the sample into \emph{college-educated} workers (BA or above, educ $>$ 110) and \emph{non-college} workers (high school or some college, educ $\leq$ 110) and estimate the main specification \eqref{eq:main} separately within each group:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:split}
Y_{it}^{\,g} = \alpha^g
  + \sum_{t \neq 2022} \beta_t^g \left( \mathbf{1}[t] \times \text{AIExposure}_i \right)
  + X_{it}'\Gamma^g + \varepsilon_{it}^g, \quad g \in \{\text{college},\;\text{non-college}\}.
\end{equation}
This approach is more transparent than a pooled regression with triple interactions: each subsample has its own baseline, trend, and exposure gradient, and the two coefficient paths $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{col}}\}$ and $\{\hat\beta_t^{\text{noncol}}\}$ can be compared directly on a combined coefficient plot. The identifying variation within each subsample is unchanged - occupation-level AI exposure interacted with year dummies - so the pre-trends are assessed separately for each group.

\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Unemployment}

Table~\ref{tab:unemployment_main} and Figure~\ref{fig:unemployment_sexeducfe_plot} present the unemployment results. The pattern is clear and remarkably stable across all three specifications (see Figures~\ref{fig:unemployment_naive_plot} and \ref{fig:unemployment_sexfe_plot} for the naive and sex-FE specifications).

In the pre-ChatGPT period, coefficients for 2018 and 2019 are statistically indistinguishable from zero. The pandemic years (2020--2021) present a noticable negative: AI-exposed occupations experienced \emph{lower} unemployment during COVID-19, with coefficients of $-0.0213^{***}$ in 2020 and $-0.0335^{***}$ in 2021. This reflects the simple fact that high-exposure occupations - legal, financial, managerial, computational - were largely remote / hybrid -compatible and thus are partly shielded from the layoffs that consentrated in the in-person service occupations.

The post-2022 reversal is the central finding. The 2023 coefficient is small and insignificant ($+0.002$), consistent with a natural lag between GPT model release and meaningful workforce adjustment. By 2024, the coefficient rises to $+0.0206^{***}$ and remains at $+0.0194^{***}$ in 2025. A 0.1 increase in exposure is associated with a 0.2 percentage point increase in unemployment probability, corresponding to roughly a 6\% increase relative to the 3.4\% baseline. This effect remains robust to controlling for sex and education composition across occupations.

\subsection{Wages}
%==================================================

Table~\ref{tab:logwage_main} presents the log-wage results. Pre-ChatGPT coefficients are uniformly small and statistically insignificant across all three specifications, confirming parallel wage trends. In the post-2022 period, the naive and sex-FE specifications yield 2025 coefficients of $-0.036$ and $-0.046$, neither significant at conventional levels. Only the most demanding specification - absorbing both sex and education fixed effects - produces a statistically significant 2025 compression of $-0.059^{**}$. No significant effect appears in 2023 or 2024 under any specification. This weak and late-emerging signal suggests that wage adjustment, if any, lags behind the employment adjustment above and is sensitive to the control set.




%==================================================
\subsection{Who Bears the Burden: Education Heterogeneity}
%==================================================

\paragraph{Unemployment.}

Splitting the sample by education reveals a symmetric picture for unemployment. Both college-educated workers (BA or above; baseline unemployment rate 2.2\%) and non-college workers (high school or some college; baseline 4.3\%) in AI-exposed occupations experienced comparable increases in unemployment risk after 2022. For college workers, the post-2022 coefficients are $+0.019^{**}$ in 2024 and $+0.021^{**}$ in 2025, stable across all three specifications. For non-college workers, the corresponding estimates are $+0.021^{***}$ in 2024 and $+0.014^{*}$ in 2025. The point estimates are similar in magnitude and the confidence intervals overlap. Hence there is no statistically significant difference in the unemployment response across education groups. Regarding pre-2022 trends: the 2018 and 2019 coefficients are near zero and insignificant for both groups. The 2020--2021 coefficients are significantly negative in both subsamples - mirroring the main-specs finding that high-exposure occupations shed less unemployment during COVID-19 - and should be read as a replication of that pattern within each education group rather than as evidence against identification. Naive and sex-FE specifications yield nearly identical results (Figures~\ref{fig:unemp_colComb_naive} and \ref{fig:unemp_colComb_sexFE}).

\paragraph{Log Wages.}

The wage results reveal a divergence across education groups. For college-educated workers, all post-2022 wage coefficients are positive and statistically insignificant: 2023 ($+0.085$), 2024 ($+0.069$), 2025 ($+0.000$). There is no evidence of wage compression among college workers in AI-exposed occupations. For non-college workers, a significant divergence emerges in 2025: the coefficient is $-0.072^{*}$ in the naive specification, rising in magnitude and significance to $-0.089^{**}$ and $-0.090^{**}$ once sex and education fixed effects are added. Pre-2022 coefficients are uniformly insignificant for both groups. The education split thus reveals a potential asymmetry: the unemployment burden of AI exposure is broadly shared, but the wage compression materializing in 2025 is concentrated among non-college workers. Naive and sex-FE specifications: Figures~\ref{fig:lw_colComb_naive} and \ref{fig:lw_colComb_sexFE}.

\subsection{Experience Heterogeneity}
%==================================================

\paragraph{Unemployment.}

Tables~\ref{tab:unemp_exphet_col} and \ref{tab:unemp_exphet_noncol} report the unemployment experience-heterogeneity results. The triple-interaction coefficients on year $\times$ experience $\times$ AI exposure are economically small and statistically insignificant across all post-2022 years and all three specifications in both subsamples. For college workers, 2024 and 2025 coefficients are approximately $+0.0007$ with standard errors of similar magnitude. For non-college workers, post-2022 estimates are even smaller (around $\pm 0.0002$) and similarly insignificant. Within either education group, there is no evidence that more-experienced workers in AI-exposed occupations faced systematically different unemployment risk.


\paragraph{Log Wages.}

The log-wage experience results are largely null: post-2022 triple-interaction coefficients are uniformly small, with only the 2025 column-(3) estimate reaching borderline significance ($-0.0076^{*}$). For non-college workers, the 2023 coefficient of $-0.0039^{**}$ suggests that more-experienced non-college workers in AI-exposed occupations faced greater wage compression immediately after ChatGPT's release, but the effect does not persist into 2024 or 2025. Interpretation is complicated by significant pre-period coefficients in 2019 ($-0.0040^{**}$) and 2021 ($-0.0060^{***}$) for the non-college subsample, indicating that the experience--AI-wage relationship was not flat before 2022 and the 2023 signal cannot be attributed cleanly to the post-ChatGPT treatment.
